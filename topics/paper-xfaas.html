The argument against dedicated infra.

"XFaaS: Hyperscale and Low Cost Serverless Functions at Meta" (SOSP 2023). At Meta scale, a single global serverless platform beats dedicated services on cost — by 7×. The paper is half engineering, half economics, and the conclusion challenges most teams' default assumption.

The claim

One global FaaS runtime, properly scheduled, can deliver functions at 1/7th the cost of dedicated service infrastructure — even with FaaS's overhead.

How it's possible

(1) Time-shifting: delay non-urgent work to off-peak hours. (2) Geo-shifting: route work to underutilised regions. (3) Right-sizing: pack many functions per host. (4) No idle: a function pays for nothing when not executing.

The scale

By 2023, XFaaS ran ~1 trillion function invocations per day across hundreds of datacenters. Internal-only; not a customer-facing product.

01Why Meta built it.

Meta's infrastructure is a fleet of millions of machines. Different services run different workloads at different rates. Most services are over-provisioned for peak load — quiet at 3am, melting at 7pm. At Meta scale, the cumulative over-provisioning is enormous; the cost line is in billions.

The XFaaS thesis: "if we move most asynchronous workloads to a unified function platform, we can globally optimise scheduling — packing work onto underutilised machines, deferring non-urgent jobs to off-peak hours, routing across regions." The unit of work shrinks from "this service's pods" to "this function invocation." The scheduler becomes the entire datacenter, not a per-service auto-scaler.

"The infrastructure win comes from the work nobody is doing right now — but is being paid for anyway."

02Time-shift & geo-shift.

Time-shifting non-urgent work

A nightly batch job, an analytics rollup, a marketing email campaign, a content-moderation re-scan — most internal asynchronous work has hours of latency tolerance. XFaaS exposes a deadline per invocation: "must finish by 6am tomorrow" or "within 1 minute." The scheduler defers tolerant work to the troughs of the daily traffic curve, runs urgent work on the peaks.

Fig. ADedicated services follow the diurnal pattern. XFaaS flattens the curve by deferring tolerant work to the troughs.

Geo-shifting across regions

Meta has datacenters in many time zones. When one region is at peak (7pm local), another is at trough (4am local). For deadline-tolerant work, route to the trough region. The latency cost is irrelevant (the work has hours of tolerance), and the cost win is huge.

The deadline-aware scheduler is the secret

Per-function deadlines are the input. The scheduler treats the global infrastructure as a constraint-satisfaction problem: maximise machine utilisation subject to per-function deadlines. Functions with tight deadlines run now; functions with loose deadlines wait. The aggregate effect is a near-flat global utilisation curve.

03Cooperative compute.

Many functions per host

A traditional service pod runs one workload. XFaaS packs many functions from many teams onto the same host, with CPU and memory isolation. Utilisation goes from ~30% (typical service) to ~80%+ (XFaaS host). The functions get along because they're scheduled to.

Function authors don't own infrastructure

Service teams worry about capacity, autoscaling, deployment, monitoring. Function authors write code and submit it. The platform owns the operational concerns. The economic argument extends: not just hardware costs, but headcount — a single platform team operates infrastructure for thousands of function-author teams.

No-idle billing model

A traditional service team pays for its pods 24/7. An XFaaS function pays for nothing when not executing. Internal chargeback reflects this — function teams' bills are proportional to actual compute consumed, not provisioned. The incentive aligns with efficiency.

05What it teaches.

Serverless is more than AWS Lambda

The "serverless is for prototypes / sparse workloads" framing is the public-cloud version. At Meta scale, the entire infrastructure is the FaaS platform. The economic argument changes when you have control of the full stack.

Deadlines are the missing scheduler input

Most workloads have implicit latency tolerance that's never expressed. If your async pipeline says "process within 1 hour" instead of "ASAP", the scheduler has options. XFaaS makes this explicit; you can do the same with Kubernetes priority classes, but most teams don't.

Cost is a function of utilisation, not technology

The 7× win comes from utilisation, not from a magic FaaS runtime. A bin-packed VM running 80% utilised is 7× cheaper than ten VMs each running 8%. The architectural lever is "pack things together"; the FaaS framing is one way to enable it.

For non-Meta teams: implications

Most teams can't build a global cooperative platform. But the principle applies: look for diurnal patterns; defer tolerant work; pack workloads onto shared hosts; measure utilisation. Kubernetes + priority classes + spot instances + cron-shifted batches gets you partway to the same shape.

The platform team is the architecture

XFaaS is a platform-engineering story. The product is the abstraction (function + deadline); the engine is global scheduling. This is the same shape as Borg / Kubernetes, taken one level higher. The next-generation infrastructure paper is, probably, more of this.

"The cost wasn't in the compute. It was in the compute that wasn't being used."
