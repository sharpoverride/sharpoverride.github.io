Design YouTube.

Two systems: an upload + transcode pipeline that processes terabytes of incoming video, and a global CDN-backed streaming service that serves billions of plays per day. The interesting choices are in transcoding strategy and storage tiering.

Anchor numbers

500 hours of video uploaded per minute. ~1B hours watched per day. ~2B logged-in users. Average video ~10 minutes.

The two halves

Upload + transcode pipeline (heavy, async). Read + stream (high-volume, CDN-served). They're separately scaled, separately bottlenecked.

Out of scope (probably)

Recommendations, search ranking, comments, monetisation. Pin scope early.

Two scale drivers: storage (PB/day forever) and egress (Tbps). The egress is what makes a CDN non-optional; you cannot serve 6 Tbps from origin.

03High-level architecture.

Fig. AUpload path is asynchronous (transcoding takes minutes). Playback path is CDN-served; metadata API is in the hot path but lightweight.

04Deep dive — upload & transcode.

Resumable chunked upload

A creator uploads a 1 GB file from a flaky mobile connection. Naive single-request upload fails on disconnect. Chunked uploads: client splits the file into 5–10 MB chunks, uploads each with its index, resumes on failure. Server tracks which chunks have landed. Same pattern as S3 multipart upload, GCS resumable upload, Tus protocol. Once all chunks land, the server stitches into the original file.

Transcoding pipeline

The original ("master") is too large and wrong-codec for most clients. Transcoding produces multiple variants: 240p, 360p, 480p, 720p, 1080p, 4K, each in H.264 and (newer) VP9/AV1 — plus thumbnail extraction, captions, watermarks. The pipeline is parallelisable: split the video into ~10-second segments, transcode each in parallel, stitch. A worker pool of GPU-accelerated transcoders handles this. A 10-minute 4K video → ~5 minutes wall-clock transcoding time across many workers.

Adaptive bitrate streaming (HLS / DASH)

After transcoding, each variant is segmented into ~6-second chunks. A manifest (M3U8 for HLS, MPD for DASH) lists all variants and their chunk URLs. The player picks a variant based on current bandwidth and switches mid-playback; this is how YouTube starts at 480p and ramps up to 1080p once the buffer fills. The chunks are the cacheable units.

Where the chunks live

Originals → cold object storage (S3 Glacier, GCS Coldline). Transcoded variants of popular videos → hot S3 / GCS, replicated to CDN. Storage tiering matters at PB/day scale: cold storage is ~$0.004/GB-month, hot is ~$0.023/GB-month. The 99% of videos rarely watched should live cold.

05Deep dive — playback.

CDN is the entire playback architecture

You cannot serve 6 Tbps of egress from your data center. Every popular chunk is replicated to CDN edge POPs around the world. Viewer hits the edge; edge has the chunk; bytes flow locally. YouTube's CDN is the largest private network outside the carriers — direct peering with ISPs (similar to Hotstar's ISP peering).

Manifest is dynamic; chunks are static

The HLS manifest is generated per-user (DRM-keyed, regional, ad-injected if applicable) and not very cacheable. The video chunks are static and cached aggressively at the edge. One M3U8 fetch costs you a hop to your data center; the 100+ chunks the viewer streams come from the local POP.

Pre-position content for predictable spikes

A new Taylor Swift video is going to be watched a billion times. Pre-warm CDN POPs with the chunks before the launch hour. Same pattern as Hotstar's pre-cached opener. The first viewer at any POP doesn't pay the origin round-trip; the content is already there.

Metadata API

"Get video by ID, return title/description/manifest URL/permissions." Lightweight, but hit on every playback start. Cached aggressively: per-video record in Redis, with the manifest URL pointing at the CDN. The metadata service handles auth, geo-restrictions, age gating before returning the URL.

06Scale & bottlenecks.

Transcoding is the bottleneck for upload

Transcoding is CPU/GPU expensive. The queue between upload and transcode is the buffer; during traffic surges, transcoding falls behind. Acceptable: a creator sees "your video is processing" for an hour. Not acceptable: viewers see "still processing" three days later.

Storage cost is the long-term concern

1 PB/day after transcoding = ~365 PB/year. Cold-tier storage at $0.004/GB-month = ~$1.5M/month per year of historical content. Storage is YouTube's biggest infrastructure cost line; the long tail of unwatched videos is the cost driver.

CDN cost vs control

Build your own CDN (YouTube's choice) or pay Cloudflare/CloudFront. Building your own pays at YouTube's scale (~$50B+ company); for everyone else, vendor CDN is correct. The break-even is around ~10 Tbps sustained.

Tradeoffs to mention

Eventual availability after upload: a freshly uploaded video isn't watchable until transcoded. Storage vs transcode time: you can transcode on-demand and not pre-encode every variant — saves storage, costs latency. Per-region storage: do you replicate the full library to every region? Probably not — replicate hot content, fall back to origin for cold.

Open questions you'd raise

Live streaming has a different architecture (low-latency HLS, faster transcoding, fewer variants). DRM (Widevine, FairPlay, PlayReady) for monetised content. Recommendations and search are entirely separate ML systems. Analytics pipeline for view counts uses sampling + approximate counters (HyperLogLog) to avoid hot-key writes.

"Two systems pretending to be one product. The interesting design choices are at the seam between them."
