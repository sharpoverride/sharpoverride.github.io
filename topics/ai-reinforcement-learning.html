RL, honestly.

Reinforcement learning is everywhere in modern AI — RLHF for LLM alignment, RL for game-playing, recommendation systems, robotics. For engineers shipping ML, you don't need to derive Bellman equations; you need to know what RL is, what it solves, and when to reach for it (rarely).

The mental model

Agent. Environment. State. Action. Reward. Policy. Optimise the policy to maximise cumulative reward. That's it.

Where it's used in production

RLHF (LLM alignment). Recommendation ranking. Ad bidding. Robotics. AlphaGo / AlphaFold. Not many other places.

01What RL solves.

Supervised learning needs labels: (input, correct output) pairs. RL needs only a reward signal: do the thing, see how much score you got. The model learns to do things that earn high scores. It's the right tool when "the correct answer" isn't well-defined, but "this output is better than that one" is.

The famous applications: game-playing (AlphaGo), robotics, ad bidding, recommendation, LLM alignment. The common thread: sequential decisions, delayed feedback, no per-step ground truth.

"Supervised learning needs a teacher. RL needs a scoreboard."

02The vocabulary.

RL algorithms — Q-learning, policy gradient, PPO, GRPO — are different ways of updating the policy to maximise expected return. You don't need to derive them to use RL; you need to know which algorithm your library uses and how to tune its key hyperparameter.

03RLHF — the LLM case.

Pre-trained LLMs predict the next token. That doesn't make them helpful, honest, or harmless. RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) is the post-training step that aligns them.

Fig. ASFT teaches "what good answers look like." The reward model teaches "what humans prefer." RL maximises preference under a KL constraint that keeps the model close to the SFT baseline.

Three steps

(1) Supervised fine-tuning (SFT): train on labeled high-quality demonstrations. The model learns the format and tone of good answers. (2) Reward model: humans rank pairs of responses; a model learns to predict the human ranking. (3) RL fine-tuning: use the reward model as scoring; optimize the policy (the LLM) with PPO (or modern variants like GRPO, DPO). The KL divergence to the SFT baseline is the regulariser — without it, the model drifts into reward-hacking.

What changed in 2024–25

DPO (Direct Preference Optimization): skips the reward model entirely; optimises preferences directly. Simpler, cheaper, comparable quality. GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization): used by DeepSeek-R1 for reasoning; samples multiple responses, scores by relative quality. The PPO-as-default era is ending.

04When to use it.

RL is the wrong default

For most ML problems, supervised learning is the right answer. RL is finicky, sample-inefficient, hard to debug. Reach for it only when you genuinely lack labels but have a reward signal.

Reward-hacking is the recurring failure

The model optimises whatever you measure, including the things you didn't mean. Click-through rewards produce clickbait; "user kept watching" rewards produce hypnotic loops. The reward is the spec; the spec is rarely right.

Bandits are RL-lite

If your problem is "pick the best option from N", multi-armed bandits give you 90% of the benefit at 10% of the complexity. Use them before reaching for full RL. Production recommendation/ranking systems use bandit-shaped algorithms (Thompson sampling, UCB) more often than full RL.

Simulation matters

RL needs many trajectories. Real-world data is expensive; simulators let you run millions. Most production RL successes (AlphaGo, robotics, ad bidding) have a high-fidelity simulator. If you can't simulate, you probably can't RL.

"RL is the answer when you can score the outcome but not annotate the steps. Most problems aren't shaped that way."

05Decision guide.

Aligning an LLM

SFT + RLHF (or DPO). Open-source libraries: TRL, OpenRLHF. Most teams don't need to do this themselves; use a frontier model API instead.

Ranking / recommendation

Start with supervised + bandits. Reach for full RL only if you have a clean offline-evaluation pipeline and a real reward signal.

Robotics / control

RL is mainstream here. Sim-to-real is the open problem. Tools: Isaac Sim, MuJoCo, Stable-Baselines3.

Game-playing

RL is the standard. AlphaZero-shaped self-play if applicable. Otherwise behavioural cloning + RL fine-tuning.

Most other ML tasks

Don't use RL. Use supervised learning, or LLM + prompt, or classical statistics. RL is the wrong default for "I have a prediction problem."

You want to learn RL anyway

Sutton & Barto is the textbook. CleanRL is the cleanest implementation reference. Build a 4-room gridworld; train PPO on CartPole; you'll learn more than from a course.
