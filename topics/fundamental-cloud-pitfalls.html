The mistakes everybody repeats.

Fifteen pitfalls that turn into Sev-2s, surprise bills, and resume-paragraph stories. None are exotic. All are documented. Most production teams ship at least three of them by accident.

The pattern

These are recurring because the cloud's defaults are not always production-safe. The vendor's docs assume you'll read every page; you won't.

01Cost pitfalls.

1. Cross-AZ egress is not free

Inter-AZ data transfer on AWS is ~$0.01/GB. A chatty service that crosses AZs constantly racks up four-figure bills. Co-locate the chatty pieces; design for AZ-affinity where possible.

2. NAT Gateway / Internet egress is the cost-line nobody sees

$0.045/GB processed plus the per-hour rate. If your serverless functions egress 10 TB/month through NAT, that's $450 in NAT alone. Use VPC endpoints for AWS-internal services; PrivateLink for vendors.

3. Forgotten dev environments

The staging cluster that's been running idle for 9 months. Auto-shutdown overnight. Schedule weekly review of unused resources. The biggest "where did our bill come from" investigations end here.

4. Reserved instances expired or wrong

An RI covering c5.xlarge but you're now on c6i.xlarge — you're paying on-demand and the RI is wasted. Audit RI utilisation monthly; modernise commitments as you modernise instances.

02Reliability pitfalls.

5. Single-AZ database

RDS single-AZ saves ~50% on cost. Loses 4–10 hours of availability during the AZ failure that happens every couple of years. Multi-AZ for anything production; the math is settled.

6. No backup of cloud-managed storage

"S3 has 11 nines of durability so we don't need backups." Doesn't protect against your own delete. Versioning + lifecycle + MFA-Delete for irreplaceable buckets. The biggest data-loss event most companies have is their own engineers, not S3.

7. Quotas you didn't know existed

EBS volumes per region. Lambda concurrent executions. RDS instances per account. Most clouds soft-cap your account. The cap matters during your highest-traffic event. Audit quotas annually; pre-request increases.

8. Region failover untested

You have multi-region for "DR"; you've never failed over. Untested DR is a fiction. Quarterly drills are the actual architecture; see Netflix Chaos.

9. Service-account key leak

The AWS access key in a commit, the GCP service-account JSON in a Slack message. Bots scan public repos in seconds; the bill arrives in hours. Detect secret commits in CI; rotate via Secret Manager; use short-lived tokens (STS, Workload Identity).

03Security pitfalls.

10. Public S3 buckets

The classic. Default has been "private" for years, but legacy buckets and "make-it-work" diagnostics still produce public-by-accident. Block-public-access at account level; the per-bucket flag is the override.

11. Overly-broad IAM

Roles with *:*. Easier than figuring out exact permissions; results in compromised tokens having keys to the kingdom. Least privilege; auto-detect overreach (AWS Access Analyzer, GCP Policy Analyzer).

12. Logging secrets

Request middleware that logs full headers. Auth header captured. Logs aggregated to Datadog. Secrets in the SIEM forever. Filter sensitive headers at the source; never trust downstream log redaction alone.

04Performance pitfalls.

13. Default HttpClient connection pool

.NET, Java, Python — the default pool sizes are tuned for low traffic. Bump pool, enable keep-alive, set PooledConnectionLifetime (see service discovery).

14. Cold starts on serverless

Lambda / Functions cold start = 100ms – 5s depending on language and image size. Hit on every period of inactivity. Provisioned concurrency for latency-sensitive functions; smaller deployment packages; native-compiled where possible.

15. Misconfigured CDN cache headers

Origin says nothing about caching; CDN serves <5% from edge. Get the Cache-Control right (see CDN chapter); it's the single biggest perf-and-cost lever for content-shaped systems.

"None of these are exotic. All of them ship anyway. The architecture's job is to inhibit the ones that don't yet."
