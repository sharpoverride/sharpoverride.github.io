Break it on a Tuesday, not a Sunday.

Netflix's Chaos Monkey randomly kills production instances during business hours. The first time you read that, it sounds insane. The realisation it forces — that your system fails the same way whether you broke it or AWS did — is the entire point of chaos engineering.

The premise

Failure in production is inevitable. The only question is whether you're testing for it during business hours, with the engineers on call, or during a 3am page no one understands.

What evolved

Chaos Monkey (kill instances) → Chaos Kong (kill regions) → ChAP (Chaos Automation Platform — controlled, targeted experiments). The tool kept getting more surgical.

The four principles

Hypothesise about steady state. Vary real-world events. Run in production. Automate & minimise blast radius. The principles, not the tools, are the export.

01Why chaos engineering exists.

Distributed systems fail in ways their architects never imagined. A network partition that lasts 800ms confuses a leader election. A clock skew of 200ms invalidates JWTs in one region. A retry storm from one upstream takes down a downstream that's been "fine for years". Most of these bugs are invisible until a real event triggers them in production; staging environments don't have the load or the topology to surface them.

Netflix's insight: "if it's going to fail anyway, fail it on purpose, with the right people watching." Run a controlled experiment during business hours. If the system stays healthy, you've validated a piece of the resilience story. If it breaks, you've found a bug that would have paged you at 3am on a worse day.

"The systems that survive partial outages are the ones that practise. The systems that don't, hope."

02The 2008 outage.

The story Netflix tells: in August 2008, a database corruption took down their DVD-shipping service for three days. The fallout: the company decided to move to AWS — and to design every subsequent service so that any single piece failing wouldn't take down the customer experience.

The Chaos Monkey emerged from that decision. If your service is going to run on cloud instances that AWS could terminate at any moment, you'd better build it to handle that — and the way to know you have is to terminate instances yourself, on a schedule, in production. The first version was a script that picked a random instance and killed it. The team running the affected service got paged. If the page was unnecessary — if the system handled the loss gracefully — they could go back to lunch. If not, they had Tuesday afternoon to fix it instead of 3am Sunday morning.

03From Chaos Monkey to ChAP.

Fig. AEach successor tool tested a coarser failure (instance → AZ → region) and ran more deliberately. ChAP is what most "chaos engineering" platforms aspire to be today.

The arc is the lesson. The first tool — Chaos Monkey — kills instances randomly, with no hypothesis. You learn something, but you might also break customer experience to learn it. The current platform — ChAP — runs controlled experiments: a small percentage of traffic, a specific service, a specific failure mode, a hypothesised outcome, a halt-on-degradation circuit breaker. Same idea, infinitely more surgical.

04The principles.

Hypothesise about steady state

Before you break anything, write down what "normal" looks like — error rates, latencies, throughput. If you can't articulate steady state, you can't tell whether you broke something. Most teams that try chaos engineering and abandon it skip this step; they "run Chaos Monkey" and conclude "the system is fine" because they didn't measure it carefully enough to see the degradation.

Vary real-world events

Test things that actually happen in production: instance kills, slow downstreams, network packet loss, clock skew, DNS lookup failures. Don't test things that don't: malicious adversaries, simultaneous loss of every node. Chaos engineering is empirical; the experiments should mirror real failure modes.

Run in production

Staging doesn't have the load, the topology, or the dependencies of production. A bug that only manifests at 100k RPS won't surface in staging. Production is where the bugs are; production is where you have to look for them. The blast-radius controls are what make this safe, not running in a separate environment.

Minimise blast radius

Start with 1% of traffic. Halt automatically if the customer-facing error rate exceeds a threshold. Have a kill switch a human can hit. Chaos engineering is the only branch of testing where the test killing customer experience is worse than the test not running at all. Get this safety net right before you scale the experiments up.

Two cohorts, one experiment. The control group sees normal behaviour; the test group sees the injected failure. Difference = signal.

06The lessons.

Measurement before manipulation

Most teams' first attempt at chaos engineering fails because they don't measure steady state precisely enough. Define your error-rate, latency, throughput baseline before you inject anything. Without a clean baseline, you can't tell that your experiment broke things — and you'll either declare success too early or roll back fixes that were working.

Start small, expand only on green

1% of traffic, one experiment at a time, automatic halt on error-rate divergence. The expansion ratchet is: green for a week → 2%, green for a week → 5%, etc. Chaos engineering shouldn't be exciting. The good outcome is "I ran my experiment, nothing happened to customers, and I learned my system handles this failure."

Practise the worst-case failover

Chaos Kong's job is region failovers. Without practice, region failover is a runbook that has never been executed, written by an engineer who left two years ago, in a system that has evolved underneath it. Practise the failover quarterly; the practice is the architecture, not the diagram.

It's a culture, not a tool

Buying a chaos-engineering SaaS doesn't give you Netflix's resilience. The cultural piece — engineers who treat the experiments as a routine part of their week, post-mortems that go to design improvements, leadership willing to sponsor "we'll fail things on purpose during business hours" — is the real artefact. The tool is two weeks of work; the culture is two years.

"Resilience isn't a feature you ship. It's a habit you keep."
