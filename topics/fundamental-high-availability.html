The nines, honestly.

"99.99% uptime" sounds great until you compute that's 53 minutes of downtime per year. SLAs, SLOs, error budgets — the vocabulary of availability is precise; the engineering follows from picking the right target.

The math

99% = 3.65 days/year down. 99.9% = 8.76 hours. 99.99% = 53 min. 99.999% = 5.26 min. Each "9" is ~10× harder.

The vocabulary

SLI: what you measure. SLO: target you commit to. SLA: contract with customers. Error budget: 1 − SLO.

Each additional 9 is ~10× the engineering investment. 99% → 99.9% is a few well-placed retries. 99.9% → 99.99% requires multi-AZ. 99.99% → 99.999% requires multi-region, careful operations, chaos engineering. The cost curve is exponential; the value curve usually isn't.

Composability is brutal. If your service depends on 5 dependencies each at 99.9%, your aggregate availability is 0.999⁵ ≈ 99.5% — half a 9 worse than any one dependency. Caching, retries, and graceful degradation are how you recover the 9s.

"The nines lie. The downtime budget tells the truth."

02SLI, SLO, SLA.

SLI — Service Level Indicator

What you measure. Examples: "fraction of HTTP requests that return 2xx", "p99 latency < 300ms", "fraction of writes that durably persist." The SLI is a number computed from telemetry.

SLO — Service Level Objective

The target. "SLI must be ≥ 99.9% over a 30-day rolling window." The SLO is the internal commitment. If you breach it, you stop shipping features and fix reliability.

SLA — Service Level Agreement

The contract with customers. "If availability drops below 99.95%, you get a refund." Always set the SLO tighter than the SLA; you want a buffer between "internally unacceptable" and "we owe you money."

Error budget

1 − SLO. For 99.9%, the error budget is 0.1% — about 43 minutes per month of "OK to be down." Spend it deliberately: a planned deploy that risks 5 min of degradation is fine if you have budget; not fine if you've already spent it on outages this month.

The cheapest 9 is the one you get from graceful degradation: don't let one feature's failure take down the whole product. Recommendations down? Show a static list. Search down? Show recent items. Most "outages" in the bad sense are full outages; partial degradation barely registers.

04Decision guide.

Internal tool, ~10 users

99% is fine. Single VM. Backups. Don't over-engineer.

B2B SaaS, paying customers

99.9% — 99.95%. Multi-AZ, retries, circuit breakers. Sub-min monitoring. Defined SLA in contract.

Payments / health / mission-critical

99.99%+. Multi-region, chaos engineering, defined error budget, dedicated SRE.

Free consumer product

99.9% is the social-media expectation. Anything less and you get bad press.

You don't know the right SLO

Start at 99.9% and measure for 90 days. If you're hitting it easily, raise. If you're missing, fix or lower.

Aiming for 99.999%

Reconsider. The engineering investment past 99.99% is enormous; the user-perceived benefit is small. Reserve for genuine safety-of-life systems.

"Five nines is a marketing number. Three nines is a real engineering target."
