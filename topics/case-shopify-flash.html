Black Friday on a monolith.

Shopify's Black Friday peak was 32 million requests per minute in 2022. The architecture serving it is a Ruby on Rails modular monolith, deployed in shards Shopify calls "pods", organised into "regions" via cell-based architecture. The interesting part is what they didn't do.

The shape

Each merchant lives in one pod. Each pod is a complete vertical stack. Flash sales never cross pod boundaries; one merchant's spike doesn't touch another's. Cell isolation is the architecture.

What they didn't do

Rewrite from Rails. Move to microservices. Migrate to a different database. The 30 TB/min Shopify monolith is the most-cited counterexample to the microservices-for-scale argument.

What the spike looks like

32M RPM = ~533k RPS. Peak GMV processed in a single minute exceeds $1M. The system is busy but never on fire because the load is partitioned.

01What flash sales do to a platform.

Shopify is a multi-tenant SaaS: hundreds of thousands of stores share one platform. The risk shape is unusual: any one store can have a flash sale at any minute — a celebrity drop, a viral product, a coordinated marketing push. The platform doesn't know which store will spike on which day. A naive multi-tenant architecture would let one store's spike consume the platform's shared capacity, degrading everyone else.

The fundamental design principle: tenants are isolated. One store's traffic does not cross into another store's compute. This is the same principle as the cell-based architecture pattern (see Cell-based architecture), applied at SaaS scale.

"Blast-radius limits are the only multi-tenant feature your customers will never know exists, and never forgive you for missing."

02Pods and cells.

Fig. AThe router resolves a store to its pod. Within the pod, the full Rails monolith handles the store's traffic with dedicated database, cache, and worker capacity.

A pod is a complete Shopify

Each pod runs the full Shopify Rails monolith — the same codebase. The pod has its own MySQL primary, its own Redis, its own background-job workers. From the application's perspective, the pod is the world: the code never queries across pods, and stores within a pod are co-tenants the same way they would have been on a single shared database.

The router knows which pod owns which store

The first hop is a request router. It looks up shop.example.com → pod-42 and forwards. The mapping is held in a small, highly-replicated configuration store. This routing decision is the only cross-pod operation in the hot path. Once you're routed to a pod, you stay there for the whole request lifecycle.

Resharding stores between pods

When a pod gets hot — too many large merchants, too much traffic — Shopify moves merchants to other pods. This is a routine operational procedure; the tooling is automated; it happens during low-traffic windows. The merchant doesn't notice unless they're in the small percentage of moves that hit a rare bug.

03The tooling around the monolith.

Throttling at the boundary

Each store has rate limits (similar to Stripe's design). The cap protects the pod from a single store consuming all its capacity. This is the inner layer of cell-isolation: even within a pod, one store's traffic is bounded.

Queue-everything-not-time-critical

Webhook deliveries, fulfillment notifications, marketing emails, analytics events — all asynchronous via background jobs. The synchronous request path stays small. During a flash sale, the queues grow; consumers catch up after the spike. The user sees a fast checkout; the downstream effects land within minutes.

Pre-defined "load shedding" tiers

Like Hotstar, Shopify has predetermined "what breaks first" tiers. Recommendations turn off. Real-time inventory previews fall back to stale-but-cached. Search may temporarily route to a degraded mode. The checkout flow is the last thing standing.

Chaos engineering — internally

Shopify runs failure injection (similar to Netflix Chaos) targeted at individual pods. The blast radius is one pod's worth of stores; the validation is that the rest of the platform keeps running. Practising the worst case is the architecture, not the diagram.

05The lessons.

Cells, not microservices

The architectural unit of scale at Shopify is a pod, not a microservice. The whole monolith runs in the pod; the partition is by tenant, not by service boundary. Cell-based architecture solves the multi-tenant blast-radius problem better than microservices does — and keeps the engineering organisation in one codebase.

Routing is the only cross-cell concern

Once a request lands in a pod, it never crosses. The router is the only piece of platform-global state, and it's a config store, not a hot path. Keep the cross-cell surface area as small as possible; every other service should run cell-locally.

Resharding tooling is non-negotiable

Stores grow at different rates. Pods get unbalanced. The ability to move a store from pod A to pod B without user impact is what makes the architecture sustainable. Build the move tooling before you need it; retrofitting it under load pressure is a multi-quarter project.

Async everything you can

Webhooks, emails, analytics, fulfillment, inventory reconciliation — all queued. The synchronous path is just "create order, confirm to user." Background workers absorb the spike; the user-facing path stays fast. If a feature can be a job, it should be a job.

A monolith can scale further than you think

Shopify is the canonical counter-example to "you have to do microservices to scale." The Rails monolith handles 533k RPS during peak. The architectural insight is the partition, not the language or the framework.

"Microservices versus monolith is the wrong question. The right question is: what's the unit of isolation, and how do you keep customers inside it?"
