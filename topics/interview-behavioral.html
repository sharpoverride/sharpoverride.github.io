Interview playbook Difficulty ★★★☆☆ Read ~11 min Stack STAR · scope · seniority signals

Stories with structure.

The behavioural interview is the most-prepared-for and least-taken-seriously round. Senior engineers fail it not because they lack stories, but because they tell them without structure. STAR is the structure. The trick is making it not sound like STAR.

What it tests

How you make decisions under pressure. How you handle conflict. How you scope. How you grow. Whether you take ownership.

The format

"Tell me about a time when…" — open-ended; ~5 minutes per answer; the interviewer drills in on details.

The standard themes

(1) Conflict. (2) Failure. (3) Influence without authority. (4) Difficult decision. (5) Cross-team project. (6) Mistake / lesson learned.

01Why it's hard for seniors.

Junior interviewers haven't been in the field long; their stories are simple and clean. Senior engineers have been in the field for a decade; their stories are massive projects with too much context. The senior failure mode is "this would take an hour to explain" — and then they spend the first three minutes on background and never get to the point.

The interviewer wants a specific shape: situation, what you did, what happened. Five minutes total. Selecting and compressing is the skill being tested, alongside the substance.

"The behavioural interview isn't about whether you've done the thing. It's about whether you can tell the story in five minutes."

02STAR, but better.

LetterWhatTime
S — Situation1–2 sentences. Project, team size, your role.30 sec
T — TaskThe specific challenge. The number that mattered.30 sec
A — ActionWhat you did. Decisions, tradeoffs, who you talked to.3 min
R — ResultWhat changed. Quantified if possible.1 min

The "A" is the entire answer

Most candidates spend too long on S and T (context-setting) and not enough on A (the actions). The interviewer scores on A. The 3 minutes on actions are where you signal seniority: how did you frame the problem? What did you consider and reject? Who did you bring along? What did you escalate and what did you handle?

Use "I", not "we"

"We migrated the database" doesn't tell the interviewer what you did. "I led the migration; I owned the rollback plan; my recommendation to do it in waves was accepted." Senior interviewers listen for "I" — not because of ego, but because they need to assess your contribution.

Quantify when you can

"It made things faster" → "p99 latency dropped from 800ms to 120ms, a 6× improvement." Specific numbers signal that you measured. "I don't remember the exact number, but it was significant" is OK occasionally; never every time.

03The themes.

Conflict (with peer / manager / staff+)

Tests: can you disagree respectfully and still get to a decision. Bad answer: "I was right, they were wrong." Good answer: "We had different priors; here's the data I brought; here's where I conceded; here's the outcome." Show that disagreement → decision, not disagreement → grudge.

Failure

Tests: ownership, growth. Bad answer: "We failed because the team didn't…". Good answer: "I made the call to X; in retrospect, I missed Y; the next time I faced a similar decision, I did Z differently." Concrete failure + concrete lesson + evidence the lesson applied.

Influence without authority

Tests: can you move an organisation. Bad: "I asked my manager to escalate." Good: "I built the prototype, brought it to staff+, scoped a 2-week experiment with team X, presented the results, secured commitment from team Y." The mechanics of getting things done in a big org.

Difficult decision

Tests: can you weigh tradeoffs explicitly. Always name the option you didn't pick, and why it was tempting. Pure-justification answers ("X was obviously right") fail; the interviewer wants to see your decision frame.

Cross-team project

Tests: can you coordinate across boundaries. The signal: do you naturally orient on team-level goals or stay siloed in your team's deliverables?

Mistake

Tests: self-awareness. The mistake should be real. Candidates who say "I work too hard" or "I take on too much" telegraph that they haven't reflected. A real mistake — and a real lesson — beats a polished non-answer.

04Anti-patterns.

"We" instead of "I"

The interviewer can't grade the team. Be specific about your contribution.

No actual decision

"And then we discussed and decided to do X." Bad. Who decided? Was it you? On what basis? The decision is the signal.

Endless setup

3 minutes of context before any action. The interviewer is glazing over. Bring the timer down: 1 minute setup, 3 minutes action, 1 minute result.

Examples too old or too small

The university project doesn't show senior judgement. The 1-day decision doesn't show scope. Pick examples that match the level you're interviewing for.

Refusing to talk about failure

"I haven't really had a failure." This is a credibility-killing answer. Everyone has failures; pick one and be honest.

"Specificity is the texture that makes you sound senior."

05Preparation guide.

Map your career into stories

List 8–12 projects. Tag each with which themes it covers (conflict / failure / influence / etc.). Aim for full coverage with overlap.

Write the bones, not the script

Bullet points for each story: situation, key actions (3–4), result. Don't memorise prose; you'll sound rehearsed.

Time yourself

5 minutes max per answer. If you go over, cut. The pruning is the practice.

Recruiter call is data

Ask what themes they emphasise. "Ownership", "bias for action", "high standards" are signals. Bring stories that hit those.

Mock with a peer

Practise with a peer who'll push back. "Why did you do that and not Y?" The follow-ups are what you're really practising.

Have your worst story ready

The one where you'd rather not talk about it. Practise telling it cleanly. If it comes up unprepared, you'll fumble; prepared, you sound mature.
