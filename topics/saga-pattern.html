Chapter 09 Difficulty ★★★★☆ Read ~16 min Stack MassTransit · RabbitMQ · Azure Service Bus · EF Core

A transaction the network can't abort.

Two-phase commit dies the moment your services live in different processes. The saga replaces the database transaction with a sequence of local transactions and a sequence of undo transactions, glued together by a state machine that survives crashes.

The problem in one line

You cannot hold a lock across order-service, payment-service, and inventory-service. The locks belong to different databases.

The thesis

A saga is a workflow with two timelines: forward steps and compensating steps. The state of the saga is the workflow's only truth — persist it, replay it, and the network can fail however it likes.

01The 2PC problem.

An order placed at POST /orders needs four things to happen, all-or-nothing: write the order row, charge the card, decrement inventory, schedule shipping. In a single SQL database, that's BEGIN TRANSACTION. Across four services with four databases, it isn't anything — there is no shared transaction manager that can ROLLBACK the payment after the shipping service times out.

The textbook answer used to be two-phase commit (2PC): a coordinator asks every participant to prepare, then to commit. In practice, 2PC holds locks across network calls. One slow participant freezes every other transaction touching those rows. One coordinator failure mid-protocol leaves participants stuck in the prepared state, waiting forever. 2PC is the reason Oracle's MTS architect quit and the reason every cloud database refuses to participate in it.

"A distributed transaction is a transaction that mostly succeeds. The remaining percent is a paging incident."

The saga, named by Garcia-Molina and Salem in a 1987 paper, is the workaround that won. Replace the single ACID transaction with a sequence of local ACID transactions, each in its own service, and define a compensating action for every step that already succeeded. If step four fails, you don't roll back — you run the inverse of step three, then step two, then step one, in reverse order. Steps commit immediately and are visible to other readers; you accept the cost of that visibility for the benefit of not holding locks.

The trick is keeping the workflow alive. Service A finishes its local commit and publishes an event. Service B is down. The event sits in a queue. When B recovers, it picks up the event and continues. The saga is the durable state of "where in the workflow are we" — and it is the only thing that needs to survive every crash.

02Orchestration vs choreography.

Two ways to wire the steps. Orchestration: one service holds the state machine and tells the others what to do next. Choreography: each service publishes "I'm done" and the next service listens for it. The diagram below is the same order workflow drawn both ways.

Fig. 05Orchestration centralises the state machine; choreography distributes it across event subscriptions. The bug surface is different, not smaller.

Why orchestration usually wins

Choreography is elegant on the whiteboard. In production, it's the workflow nobody can describe. Adding a fifth step means hunting through five repositories to find every handler, every event subscription, every retry policy. Three months in, somebody adds a "tax-calculation" service between payment and inventory and the system silently skips it for half the traffic because the inventory handler still listens for CardCharged.

Use orchestration unless you have a specific reason not to. Specific reasons: you really do have a fan-out style domain where a hundred listeners care about OrderPlaced for analytics, fraud, recommendations, marketing emails. There, choreography is the right primitive. For the core transactional workflow, the orchestrator is the source of truth and the audit log.

The state lives in a database

Whichever shape you pick, somebody must persist the workflow's state. With orchestration, the saga instance is a row in Postgres / Cosmos / SQL with a current-state column and a per-instance correlation id. With choreography, each service keeps its own slice of the state and you reassemble the whole picture from event-sourcing or distributed-tracing telemetry. The orchestrator's state row is the difference between an audit trail you can read and a debugging session that starts in Application Insights and ends at the airport.

03Compensation is not rollback.

Inventory has decremented. Payment has charged. Shipping returns "no carrier serves that postcode." There is no ROLLBACK button — the payment is already in the bank's ledger, the stock is already off the shelf. The compensating action is a new business operation: refund the card, re-increment the stock. It is observable. The customer sees the charge appear and disappear. Auditors will ask why.

The undo path is a first-class workflow with the same retry, persistence, and observability guarantees as the forward path.

Compensations must be idempotent

The refund will be retried. The restock will be retried. They run on the same broker with the same at-least-once guarantee as everything else. Every compensation needs a stable correlation id (the saga instance id is the natural choice) and a check at the start: have I already refunded this saga? Without that check, a transient network blip turns one refund into two, and the customer notices.

Some steps cannot be undone

You can refund a card. You cannot un-send an email. You cannot un-ship a parcel once the courier has scanned it. The mitigation is ordering: put the un-undoable steps last. The last forward step doesn't need a compensation — by the time you get to it, every prior step has succeeded. If shipping is the last step and shipping fails, the saga aborts with full rollback. If notification is the last step and notification fails, that's a logging incident, not a $40 refund.

Semantic locks

Between forward and compensation, the data is in a half-committed state. The order is "pending payment", the stock is "reserved but not shipped". Other readers see this state. The fix is a semantic lock: a status column that downstream queries respect. Reports filter on Status = 'Confirmed', not Status IN ('Pending', 'Confirmed'). The saga moves the row from Pending → Confirmed atomically as the last step. It's the database equivalent of "released to production".

04In MassTransit.

MassTransit's MassTransitStateMachine<T> is the closest .NET has to a saga toolkit. The state machine is declarative, the instance is an EF Core entity persisted with optimistic concurrency, and the broker is whichever you've already wired up — RabbitMQ, Azure Service Bus, Amazon SQS. The example below is the full order saga: four forward steps, four compensations, one timeout.

C#   OrderSaga.cs 
// dotnet add package MassTransit.EntityFrameworkCore
using MassTransit;

namespace Academy.Sagas;

public sealed class OrderSagaState : SagaStateMachineInstance, ISagaVersion
{
    public Guid CorrelationId { get; set; }
    public int Version { get; set; }      // optimistic concurrency
    public string CurrentState { get; set; } = "";
    public Guid CustomerId { get; set; }
    public decimal Amount     { get; set; }
    public Guid?   PaymentId  { get; set; }
    public Guid?   ReservationId { get; set; }
}

public sealed class OrderSaga : MassTransitStateMachine<OrderSagaState>
{
    public State AwaitingPayment { get; private set; } = null!;
    public State AwaitingStock   { get; private set; } = null!;
    public State AwaitingShip    { get; private set; } = null!;
    public State Completed       { get; private set; } = null!;
    public State Compensating    { get; private set; } = null!;
    public State Failed          { get; private set; } = null!;

    public Event<OrderSubmitted>  Submitted       { get; private set; } = null!;
    public Event<PaymentCharged>  PaymentDone     { get; private set; } = null!;
    public Event<PaymentFailed>   PaymentBad      { get; private set; } = null!;
    public Event<StockReserved>   StockDone       { get; private set; } = null!;
    public Event<StockUnavailable> StockBad        { get; private set; } = null!;
    public Event<ShipmentBooked>  ShipDone        { get; private set; } = null!;
    public Event<ShipmentFailed>  ShipBad         { get; private set; } = null!;
    public Schedule<OrderSagaState, PaymentTimeout> PaymentDeadline
        { get; private set; } = null!;

    public OrderSaga()
    {
        InstanceState(x => x.CurrentState);

        Event(() => Submitted, x => x.CorrelateById(m => m.Message.OrderId));
        Event(() => PaymentDone, x => x.CorrelateById(m => m.Message.OrderId));
        Event(() => StockDone,   x => x.CorrelateById(m => m.Message.OrderId));
        Event(() => ShipDone,    x => x.CorrelateById(m => m.Message.OrderId));

        Schedule(() => PaymentDeadline, x => x.PaymentTimeoutTokenId, s =>
        {
            s.Delay = TimeSpan.FromMinutes(5);
            s.Received(e => e.CorrelateById(m => m.Message.OrderId));
        });

        Initially(
            When(Submitted)
                .Then(ctx =>
                {
                    ctx.Saga.CustomerId = ctx.Message.CustomerId;
                    ctx.Saga.Amount     = ctx.Message.Amount;
                })
                .Publish(ctx => new ChargeCard(
                    ctx.Saga.CorrelationId, ctx.Saga.CustomerId, ctx.Saga.Amount))
                .Schedule(PaymentDeadline, ctx => new PaymentTimeout(ctx.Saga.CorrelationId))
                .TransitionTo(AwaitingPayment));

        During(AwaitingPayment,
            When(PaymentDone)
                .Then(ctx => ctx.Saga.PaymentId = ctx.Message.PaymentId)
                .Unschedule(PaymentDeadline)
                .Publish(ctx => new ReserveStock(ctx.Saga.CorrelationId))
                .TransitionTo(AwaitingStock),
            When(PaymentBad).TransitionTo(Failed),
            When(PaymentDeadline.Received).TransitionTo(Failed));

        During(AwaitingStock,
            When(StockDone)
                .Then(ctx => ctx.Saga.ReservationId = ctx.Message.ReservationId)
                .Publish(ctx => new BookShipment(ctx.Saga.CorrelationId))
                .TransitionTo(AwaitingShip),
            When(StockBad)
                .Publish(ctx => new RefundPayment(
                    ctx.Saga.CorrelationId, ctx.Saga.PaymentId!.Value))
                .TransitionTo(Compensating));

        During(AwaitingShip,
            When(ShipDone).TransitionTo(Completed).Finalize(),
            When(ShipBad)
                .Publish(ctx => new ReleaseStock(
                    ctx.Saga.CorrelationId, ctx.Saga.ReservationId!.Value))
                .Publish(ctx => new RefundPayment(
                    ctx.Saga.CorrelationId, ctx.Saga.PaymentId!.Value))
                .TransitionTo(Compensating));

        SetCompletedWhenFinalized();
    }
}

Wire the saga into the bus with EF Core persistence. The repository is the durable state; the DbContext is yours, with one table for instances and an outbox table to keep "publish" and "save state" in the same transaction.

C#   Program.cs 
var builder = WebApplication.CreateBuilder(args);

builder.Services.AddDbContext<SagaDbContext>(o =>
    o.UseSqlServer(builder.Configuration.GetConnectionString("Sagas")));

builder.Services.AddMassTransit(x =>
{
    x.AddSagaStateMachine<OrderSaga, OrderSagaState>()
     .EntityFrameworkRepository(r =>
     {
         r.ConcurrencyMode = ConcurrencyMode.Optimistic;   // row-version check
         r.ExistingDbContext<SagaDbContext>();
         r.UseSqlServer();
     });

    x.AddPublishMessageScheduler();    // for the 5-minute payment deadline
    x.AddEntityFrameworkOutbox<SagaDbContext>(o =>
    {
        o.UseSqlServer();
        o.UseBusOutbox();   // publish-with-save-changes, transactional
    });

    x.UsingAzureServiceBus((ctx, cfg) =>
    {
        cfg.Host(builder.Configuration.GetConnectionString("ServiceBus"));
        cfg.UseMessageScheduler(new Uri("queue:scheduler"));
        cfg.ConfigureEndpoints(ctx);
    });
});

var app = builder.Build();
app.MapPost("/orders", async (SubmitOrder req, IPublishEndpoint bus) =>
{
    var orderId = Guid.NewGuid();
    await bus.Publish(new OrderSubmitted(orderId, req.CustomerId, req.Amount));
    return Results.Accepted($"/orders/{orderId}", new { orderId });
});
app.Run();

05The detail.

Always use the transactional outbox

Without it, you do save state, then publish, and a crash between the two means the saga is in a state nobody listens to anymore. With UseBusOutbox, the published message is written to a SQL table in the same transaction as the state change, and a background dispatcher drains the outbox onto the broker. The saga is consistent with the wire by construction.

Optimistic concurrency on the instance

Two events for the same saga can arrive concurrently — a slow StockReserved and a fast StockUnavailable from a retry, for example. ConcurrencyMode.Optimistic uses the row's version column; the second writer fails and MassTransit re-runs the message. Without it, you'll see "lost updates" — a state transition silently overwritten by a parallel one.

Every step is its own deadline

The 5-minute payment scheduler in the sample is one of typically four or five. Every awaited event needs a timeout, because no event = silent saga = forever-pending order. Tune the deadlines from real payment / inventory / shipping p99 latency, with a 3× safety margin. Without timeouts, a hung downstream is invisible until a customer complains a week later.

Compensations are messages, not method calls

The saga doesn't call RefundService.Refund(...) in-process — it publishes a RefundPayment message. The refund service consumes it with its own retry, its own circuit breaker, its own DLQ. The saga doesn't await the refund; it transitions to Compensating and forgets. When the refund's PaymentRefunded event lands, the saga handles it and either finalizes or moves to the next compensation. The asynchrony is the entire point.

Don't reach for sagas if you have one database

If "order, payment, inventory, shipping" all live in the same SQL Server, a regular transaction is correct and a saga is the wrong shape. Sagas pay for themselves when service boundaries are real and the data is genuinely partitioned. The line is one database per workflow step, not one bounded context per workflow step.

"The saga is the only artifact in your codebase that survives a 3am restart of every service mid-checkout. Treat it like the priority-zero asset it is."

07Decision guide.

3–5 microservices on RabbitMQ / Service Bus

MassTransit saga with EF Core repository. Optimistic concurrency, transactional outbox, scheduled timeouts. The .NET default for a reason.

Greenfield with no broker yet

Start with Azure Service Bus + MassTransit. Don't roll your own. The broker, the dedupe, and the DLQ are the boring parts you don't want to write.

Polyglot stack (C# + Go + Node)

Temporal.io. The SDKs are first-class in every major language, and the workflow definition lives in code in each repo.

Already serverless on Azure

Durable Functions. The orchestrator function reads like a regular method; the durability is invisible. Cheap until you hit millions of activations.

Fan-out, not a transaction

Choreography is fine: publish OrderPlaced, let analytics, fraud, marketing, recommendations all consume it. No saga needed — these aren't compensable.

All four services share one database

Stop. Use a regular SQL transaction. A saga adds operational cost for a problem you don't have.

Three things people get wrong

One — no timeout on awaited events. The downstream goes silent, the saga sits in AwaitingPayment forever, the customer pages support. Every awaited state needs a scheduled deadline and a transition to Failed.

Two — compensating with a method call. "Just call refundService.Refund(...) in the catch block." That call dies with the pod that owns the saga and your customer is now charged with no refund. Compensations are messages on the same durable bus as forward steps.

Three — treating the saga as a transaction. Other readers see the in-progress state. Reports include the half-built order. Stock briefly shows reserved-but-not-shipped. Use semantic-lock status columns and design your queries accordingly.
