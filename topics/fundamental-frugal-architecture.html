Cost is a non-functional requirement.

Werner Vogels' 2024 manifesto: cost belongs on the architecture diagram. Most teams optimise for correctness and performance; cost is what the CFO worries about. Frugal architecture treats cost as a first-class design dimension — alongside latency, throughput, availability.

The seven laws

(1) Make cost a non-functional requirement. (2) Systems that last align cost to business. (3) Architecting is a series of trade-offs. (4) Unobserved systems lead to unknown costs. (5) Cost-aware architectures implement cost controls. (6) Cost optimisation is incremental. (7) Unchallenged success leads to assumptions.

01Why cost matters now.

Cloud was sold as "infinite elasticity"; the implicit promise was that capacity wasn't the constraint. The bill was. Many teams spent years optimising for development speed and ignored unit-economics. Then came the era of "wait, our compute bill is bigger than our salary spend."

Werner Vogels' framing: cost belongs on the same dashboard as latency. An architecture that achieves 50ms p99 at $100/req is failing. The CFO is a stakeholder; the architecture is also their problem.

"Cost-of-goods-sold is a feature. Make it a feature you measure."

02The seven laws.

1. Cost is a non-functional requirement

Put it in the architecture review. "What's the cost per request?" should be as common as "what's the p99 latency?"

2. Systems that last align cost to business

A system where cost-per-request scales with revenue-per-request is sustainable. One where cost grows faster than revenue is a clock running down. Per-user revenue × usage = per-user revenue; per-user infra cost should scale similarly.

3. Architecting is a series of trade-offs

Cost is always a trade. Cheaper storage = colder reads. Spot instances = potential preemption. More cache = staler data. Name the trade explicitly; don't pretend the cheap option is free.

4. Unobserved systems lead to unknown costs

If you can't tag your spend by feature/team/customer, you can't optimise. Cost observability is engineering: tag resources, attribute spend, dashboard it. AWS Cost Explorer, GCP Billing, Azure Cost Management — they all support tagging.

5. Cost-aware architectures implement cost controls

Limits, alerts, kill-switches. An unbounded queue is also an unbounded bill. An unbounded log is an unbounded storage tier. Build limits in.

6. Cost optimisation is incremental

Most savings come from many small wins. Right-sizing instances. Stopping idle dev environments. Switching to spot. Reducing log retention. Don't wait for a 10× rewrite; ship a 5% saving today.

7. Unchallenged success leads to assumptions

"We've always used Redshift" doesn't mean Redshift is still the right choice. Periodically audit your stack: would we pick this today? Architectures decay against the moving frontier of cloud offerings.

04Decision guide.

Your cloud bill is climbing >30% YoY

Allocate engineering time to cost. Audit tagging; identify the top 5 line items; pick 2 to optimise per quarter.

Building greenfield

Add a "cost per request" cell to every architecture-review template. Treat it like p99.

Pre-launch SaaS

Model the unit economics. Per-customer infra cost vs per-customer revenue. The math has to work at every plan tier.

Burst-y workload

Spot / preemptible instances are massively cheaper. The interruption is bearable for batch; build the work to checkpoint.

Lots of stored data

Storage tiering. S3 Standard → S3 IA → Glacier. ~10× savings on cold data with managed lifecycle policies.

FinOps maturity

FinOps is the discipline. Hire / promote someone whose job is the cloud bill. The ROI is high.

"The cheapest watt is the one you didn't burn. Same goes for the cheapest dollar."
