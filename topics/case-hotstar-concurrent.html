Case study Year 2023 Scale 59M concurrent (peak) Stack CDN sharding · pre-warmed capacity · graceful degradation

59 million viewers. One cricket match.

Disney+ Hotstar holds the world record for concurrent streaming viewers — 59 million during the 2023 ICC World Cup. The previous record (its own, 2019) was 25 million. The architecture isn't elastic; it's pre-positioned. You don't auto-scale for 59 million; you reserve.

The shape of the spike

0 to 25M concurrent in <5 minutes when a marquee match starts. Auto-scaling cannot fill that gap. The capacity has to be there at minute zero.

The trick

Pre-warm everything. Shard the CDN by ISP and geography. Degrade gracefully — comments, chat, emoji can disappear; the video keeps playing.

The math

59M users × 4 Mbps average = ~236 Tbps peak egress. For comparison, Netflix's global peak is in similar territory. Hotstar achieves it during one match.

0159M is not auto-scaling.

Auto-scaling assumes you can spin up capacity in minutes. A pod cold-starts in ~30 seconds; a new VM in ~3 minutes; a new region in ~30 minutes. Cricket viewers don't wait. A live broadcast starts at a known minute, and tens of millions of viewers connect simultaneously. The capacity ramp from 0 to 25 million happens in five minutes. You cannot spin up an engineering organisation's worth of compute in five minutes from cold.

The Hotstar pattern: pre-warm everything before kick-off. Capacity is already running. CDN POPs are already cached with the start-of-broadcast content. Auth servers have already mint-cached short-lived tokens. The traffic surge meets a system that's been ready for an hour. Auto-scaling exists only to handle the upside surprise — the surge beyond the forecast.

"Elastic scale is a marketing term. Live sports is the workload that proves it false."

02Pre-positioned capacity.

Fig. AViewers ramp from zero to 25M in 5 minutes. The capacity to serve them has been running, paid for, idle, for the previous hour.

Pre-cache at the edge

The first 30 seconds of the broadcast — the opening credits, the toss — are pre-cached at every Hotstar POP an hour before kick-off. The first 25M HLS playlist requests get the same cached chunks; no origin fetch, no cold-start. Without this, even with capacity reserved, the origin would melt in the first 60 seconds.

Shard the CDN by ISP

India's broadcast traffic flows through a small number of large ISPs (Jio, Airtel, BSNL). Hotstar peers directly with each ISP and serves traffic from POPs inside the ISP's network. This avoids the public-internet middle for the last mile; the bytes travel within the ISP's own backbone. The savings on transit cost and latency are both real and large.

03Graceful degradation.

Not every feature has to survive the spike. The video has to keep playing. Everything else is optional. Hotstar built explicit degradation tiers:

FeatureP0What degrades first
Video playbackCriticalBitrate downgrades before drops
Login / sign-upCriticalTokens cached aggressively
Player controlsImportantSeek may stall
Comments / chatOptionalDisabled at overload
Emoji reactionsOptionalDisabled at overload
Watch-partyOptionalDisabled at overload
RecommendationsOptionalFall back to cached static list

The product team accepted this in advance. Engineering didn't make the call mid-incident. Pre-agreed degradation matrices are an architectural artefact; if you have to decide what to break at 11pm during a match, you've already lost.

04The 5 billion emoji problem.

In 2019 Hotstar published a follow-up: 5 billion emoji reactions delivered in real time during a single match. The naive shape would have been one WebSocket message per emoji per viewer — 25M viewers × any per-second rate × N friends = impossible. The trick was aggregation at the broker: count emoji reactions in 1-second windows, broadcast the aggregate to all viewers, render as an animated overlay. Each viewer sees an emoji storm; no viewer sees any individual emoji's per-message delivery.

The compute moves from "deliver N messages to N viewers" (N² fan-out) to "deliver 1 aggregate message to N viewers" (N fan-out). For 25M concurrent viewers, that's the difference between viable and impossible.

06The lessons.

Pre-position; don't auto-scale

For predictable traffic events (scheduled broadcasts, product launches, election results), capacity must be running before the surge. Auto-scaling fills the gap above the forecast; it cannot serve the surge itself. The first hour after kick-off is paid for in advance.

Cache the first 30 seconds

Whatever the first minute of your spike looks like, cache it everywhere. Hotstar caches the broadcast opener at every POP; you should cache the most-likely-to-be-fetched-first content at your edge. The first 30 seconds of a flash sale is the same content for every visitor.

Negotiate degradation tiers before the event

Decide what breaks first, second, third, before the launch. Get sign-off from product. Wire the degradation in code so it activates by automated signal — not by an engineer's pager. If degradation is a decision you make during the incident, you've already failed.

Aggregate at the broker, not at the client

The emoji-fan-out problem is the same shape as Twitch's chat, Slack's reactions, any "many → many" real-time UI. Aggregate at the producer side, broadcast the rollup; don't fan out per-message to every consumer. The viewer's brain interprets a 60-emoji-per-second stream the same as a 6000-emoji-per-second one.

Peer with the last-mile ISPs

Bytes travelling on the public internet are expensive bytes. Bytes travelling within the ISP's network are cheap. For markets where traffic concentrates in a few ISPs, direct peering — colocating POPs inside the ISP's data centres — is the cost win that nobody talks about because it requires business deals, not engineering.

"The hardest spike isn't the one you didn't see coming. It's the one you knew the exact minute of and still failed to prepare for."
