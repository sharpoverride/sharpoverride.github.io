Write somewhere, repair later.

A node is down. A write needs to land somewhere. Hinted handoff: store the write on a healthy peer with a note saying "this belongs to node X"; when X recovers, the peer delivers. The result: writes accept during partitions; consistency converges when the partition heals.

What it solves

Strict quorum writes block when a replica is down. Hinted handoff keeps writes accepting at the cost of brief staleness.

Where it lives

Dynamo (2007), Cassandra, Riak, ScyllaDB. The technique is the canonical "AP" survival pattern.

01Strict quorum's fragility.

You have N replicas. Configured for W=2 writes to succeed. If one replica is down and another is slow, W=2 can't be satisfied; the write rejects. The user sees an error; the system technically had capacity to absorb the write somewhere; it just refused.

For a payment, this might be acceptable — you'd rather a clear error than ambiguity. For "add to cart," it's a customer-experience disaster. Hinted handoff lets the write succeed against a stand-in replica, deferring delivery to the intended node.

"The strict-quorum write that blocks is also the strict-quorum write that loses a sale."

02How it works.

Cluster of 5 nodes; replication factor 3; for key K the canonical replicas are nodes {A, B, C}. A is down. Coordinator writes to B and C as normal; for A's replica, it picks the next healthy node D and writes there with a hint: "this data belongs to A; deliver when A recovers."

Sloppy quorum + hinted handoff: W is satisfied against any 2 reachable nodes, not specifically A/B/C. The write succeeds. The hint sits on D until A comes back, then drains.

Recovery loop

Every minute (or so), D checks: is A reachable? If yes, send the pending hints. A applies them; D deletes them from its hint log. Eventually consistent: after A recovers, the data flows in, the cluster converges.

Anti-entropy is the backstop

Hints can be lost (D itself crashes; hints expire). Periodic anti-entropy — Merkle-tree comparison between replicas — detects and repairs any remaining divergence. Hinted handoff is the fast path; anti-entropy is the safety net.

03Tradeoffs.

Brief inconsistency

Reads against A immediately after recovery may not see the hinted writes (yet). Read quorum (R=2) covers it: at least one of B or C has the data. Read-your-own-writes survives if R+W > N.

Hint storms

If A is down for an hour, D accumulates an hour of writes. When A returns, D dumps everything; A's network and disk spike. Tune the drain rate to avoid melting the recovering node.

Hints expire

By policy (typically 3–24 hours). If A doesn't recover in that window, hints are dropped. Don't rely on hints for long-outage recovery; use the anti-entropy mechanism (read repair, periodic Merkle compare).

Not for safety-critical data

The window of inconsistency is real. For payments or accounting, prefer strict-quorum and accept the availability cost. Hinted handoff is the AP-style "writes always accept" answer.

04Decision guide.

AP-style database (Cassandra, Riak)

Hinted handoff on by default. Tune hint TTL and drain rate by your operational reality.

CP-style database (Spanner, Zookeeper)

Not used. Strict consensus refuses writes during partition; that's the correct semantics for the workload.

Custom replication layer

Worth implementing for shopping-cart-shaped workloads. Skip for ledger-shaped workloads.

Need both consistency and availability

You can't have both during partition (CAP). Hinted handoff gives availability and converges to consistency; pure linearisability gives the inverse. Pick by data semantics.

Long-outage recovery

Anti-entropy (Merkle trees) is the backstop. Hints expire; entropy detection doesn't.

Hot key during outage

One downed replica + a viral content burst = the standby gets crushed. Rate-limit the drain when the original replica returns.

"Hinted handoff is the technique that turns 'sometimes-down' into 'never blocked'."
