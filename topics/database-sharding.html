Shard once. Reshard never.

A single Postgres can hold more than you think and less than you'll need. The honest version of range vs. hash vs. directory, plus the resharding move that Figma made famous — wired to EF Core, not whiteboard.

The problem in one line

The write path is the wall. Reads scale with replicas; writes scale with shards. One leader, finite IOPS.

The thesis

Pick a sharding scheme you can live with for 5 years, not one that's clever today. The cost of resharding is the cost of the system you replace, not the system you have.

01The single-leader wall.

Cloudflare runs 55M requests-per-second on 15 Postgres clusters. Figma served 4M users on Postgres alone before they sharded. Most teams shard a year too early because a graph in a conference slide scared them. Some shard a year too late because they trusted that same graph.

The wall has a shape. Read traffic scales linearly with replicas — add a follower, double your read capacity, your data is at most a second stale. Write traffic does not. Every transaction goes through one leader, and one leader has finite IOPS, finite WAL bandwidth, and one CPU core for the commit path. That is the wall. Before you hit it, you don't need sharding. After you hit it, no amount of caching saves you.

"You can buy your way out of the read problem with a bigger instance. The write problem you have to solve."

Concrete numbers. A db.r6g.16xlarge on Aurora Postgres (64 vCPU, 512 GB RAM, $9.5k/mo) sustains ~40,000 simple transactions per second on a well-tuned workload before write latency degrades. Azure SQL Hyperscale Business Critical at 80 vCPU pushes ~30,000 TPS at ~$22k/mo. Past that number, you don't get bigger boxes — you get more boxes, and you split the data across them. That split is what sharding is.

Before we go further: partitioning and sharding are not the same thing. Partitioning splits one table inside one database. Sharding splits one database into many databases. The first is a Postgres feature you should use whenever you have a table over 100M rows. The second is an architecture you should adopt only when you have no choice.

02Three schemes.

Three ways to decide which row goes to which shard. Each is a different tradeoff between query patterns, rebalancing pain, and how often you have to think about it.

Fig. 01Three shard-key schemes, side by side. The pros and cons travel with you for the lifetime of the system.

Range sharding

Pick a column, slice it into ranges, send each range to a shard. Range scans are cheap — a query for user_id BETWEEN 1000 AND 2000 hits exactly one shard. This is what Postgres native partitioning does. It's also what every "I'll just add a shard column" first attempt ends up looking like.

The failure mode is famous. If your shard key correlates with time — auto-incrementing IDs, timestamps, anything monotonic — the newest shard takes all the write traffic. Twitter learned this with their first sharding pass. Discord learned it before they moved to Cassandra. The fix is to choose a key that isn't time-correlated, or to accept that you'll be splitting the hottest shard every quarter.

Hash sharding

Pick a column, hash it, take the result modulo the shard count. Even distribution by design. This is the default choice for OLTP workloads. Cosmos DB uses it. DynamoDB uses it. Vitess uses it. Almost every internally-developed sharded system at FAANG uses it.

The cost: range queries become scatter-gather. WHERE created_at BETWEEN ... AND ... visits every shard. The other cost — and this is the one that sinks teams — is that naive modulo hashing rebalances every key when you add a shard. Go from 4 shards to 5, and ~80% of your keys move. This is the chapter on consistent hashing — go read it if you haven't, then come back.

Directory sharding

Maintain a lookup table: tenant 42 lives on shard B. Shopify's per-pod isolation, Slack's per-workspace sharding, Notion's per-workspace sharding. The pattern is "one customer never spans two shards, and we move customers individually when one gets too big."

You pay for it with a directory lookup on every request — typically cached in the application, refreshed on miss. The win is that you can move a single tenant from a hot shard to a cold one in minutes, without touching anyone else's data. For multi-tenant SaaS this is usually the right answer.

03The resharding problem.

Picking a shard scheme is the easy part. Surviving the day you have to add shards is the hard part. Three approaches that work in production, in increasing order of investment.

Six steps. No downtime, no data loss, two months of careful work. The verifier step is the one nobody plans for and everyone needs.

Pre-shard to virtual partitions

The cheapest move is to pick a large number of logical shards up front and map them to fewer physical shards. Pre-allocate 1,024 logical shards across 4 physical machines (256 each). When you outgrow that, add a fifth machine and move 200 logical shards onto it. The application uses the logical shard ID; the mapping changes underneath.

This is what Vitess, Citus, and Cosmos DB all do. You should do it too on day one, before you have any data. The cost is zero, and you've bought yourself the option not to reshard for the lifetime of the system.

Online dual-write (Figma's move)

When pre-sharding wasn't done — and most systems weren't pre-sharded — the standard move is dual-write. The application writes every change to both the old shard and the new shard. A backfill job copies historical rows in batches, idempotently. A verifier reads from both sides and compares. When the dual-write window is clean and the verifier finds zero diffs, you flip reads to the new shard, then flip off the old write. Figma's "How we scaled Postgres" writeup is the canonical version. Two months end to end, zero downtime.

The thing nobody plans for: the verifier. A backfill that "looks complete" is the most expensive bug in a sharded system. You need a daemon that reads a sample of rows from old, reads the corresponding rows from new, and pages you when they disagree. Run it for the full dual-write window.

Just use a sharded database

Citus on Postgres or Vitess on MySQL turn sharding from an application problem into a database problem. You write SQL with a shard key; the gateway routes. Add a worker; it rebalances. This is the path Stripe, Slack, GitHub, and Quora ended up on. If you're starting fresh in 2026, this is the answer.

"Most teams who say they sharded actually directory-sharded. Most teams who think they hash-sharded actually mod-sharded. The labels are sloppy. The bills are not."

04In .NET.

If you're not on Citus / Cosmos DB / Vitess and you have to roll application-level sharding, here's the pattern that wears well. A ShardRouter picks the shard from a key, an IDbContextFactory<TenantDbContext> per shard hands out connections, and a small middleware enriches every request with its tenant — so EF Core lookups route automatically. Consistent hashing under the hood, so adding shards moves O(K/N) keys instead of O(K).

C#   ShardRouter.cs 
// dotnet add package Microsoft.EntityFrameworkCore.SqlServer
// dotnet add package Microsoft.EntityFrameworkCore.Design
using System.Collections.Concurrent;
using System.Security.Cryptography;
using System.Text;
using Microsoft.EntityFrameworkCore;

namespace Academy.Sharding;

public sealed record ShardDescriptor(string Id, string ConnectionString);

public interface IShardRouter
{
    ShardDescriptor Pick(string tenantKey);
    IReadOnlyList<ShardDescriptor> All { get; }
}

// Consistent-hash ring with 200 virtual nodes per shard.
// Adding a shard moves ~1/N of keys, not all of them.
public sealed class ConsistentHashShardRouter : IShardRouter
{
    private readonly SortedDictionary<uint, ShardDescriptor> _ring = new();
    private readonly uint[] _sortedKeys;
    private const int VirtualNodes = 200;

    public ConsistentHashShardRouter(IEnumerable<ShardDescriptor> shards)
    {
        All = shards.ToArray();
        if (All.Count == 0)
            throw new ArgumentException("At least one shard required.");

        foreach (var shard in All)
            for (var v = 0; v < VirtualNodes; v++)
                _ring[Hash($"{shard.Id}#{v}")] = shard;

        _sortedKeys = _ring.Keys.ToArray();
    }

    public IReadOnlyList<ShardDescriptor> All { get; }

    public ShardDescriptor Pick(string tenantKey)
    {
        if (string.IsNullOrWhiteSpace(tenantKey))
            throw new ArgumentException("tenantKey is required.");

        var h = Hash(tenantKey);
        var idx = Array.BinarySearch(_sortedKeys, h);
        if (idx < 0) idx = ~idx;
        if (idx == _sortedKeys.Length) idx = 0;
        return _ring[_sortedKeys[idx]];
    }

    private static uint Hash(string input)
    {
        var bytes = MD5.HashData(Encoding.UTF8.GetBytes(input));
        return BitConverter.ToUInt32(bytes, 0);
    }
}

// Hands out an EF Core context bound to the right shard.
public sealed class TenantDbContextFactory
{
    private readonly IShardRouter _router;
    private readonly ConcurrentDictionary<string, DbContextOptions<TenantDbContext>> _opts = new();

    public TenantDbContextFactory(IShardRouter router) => _router = router;

    public TenantDbContext For(string tenantKey)
    {
        var shard = _router.Pick(tenantKey);
        var opts = _opts.GetOrAdd(shard.Id, _ =>
            new DbContextOptionsBuilder<TenantDbContext>()
                .UseSqlServer(shard.ConnectionString, sql =>
                {
                    sql.EnableRetryOnFailure(maxRetryCount: 3);
                    sql.CommandTimeout(15);
                })
                .Options);
        return new TenantDbContext(opts);
    }
}

Now the minimal API side — middleware reads the tenant header, picks the shard, and stuffs the right DbContext into the request scope:

C#   Program.cs 
var builder = WebApplication.CreateBuilder(args);

// Wire up shards from configuration (rotate via Key Vault, hot reload via IOptionsMonitor).
var shards = builder.Configuration
    .GetSection("Shards")
    .Get<ShardDescriptor[]>()
    ?? throw new InvalidOperationException("Shards section missing.");

builder.Services.AddSingleton<IShardRouter>(new ConsistentHashShardRouter(shards));
builder.Services.AddSingleton<TenantDbContextFactory>();
builder.Services.AddScoped(sp =>
{
    var http   = sp.GetRequiredService<IHttpContextAccessor>().HttpContext!;
    var tenant = http.Request.Headers["X-Tenant"].ToString();
    if (string.IsNullOrEmpty(tenant))
        throw new UnauthorizedAccessException("Missing X-Tenant header.");
    return sp.GetRequiredService<TenantDbContextFactory>().For(tenant);
});
builder.Services.AddHttpContextAccessor();

var app = builder.Build();

app.MapGet("/orders/{id:guid}", async (Guid id, TenantDbContext db, CancellationToken ct) =>
{
    var order = await db.Orders.FindAsync(new object[] { id }, ct);
    return order is null ? Results.NotFound() : Results.Ok(order);
});

app.MapPost("/orders", async (Order input, TenantDbContext db, CancellationToken ct) =>
{
    db.Orders.Add(input);
    await db.SaveChangesAsync(ct);
    return Results.Created($"/orders/{input.Id}", input);
});

app.Run();

Every request resolves a TenantDbContext bound to the right shard. The application code looks like it's hitting one database. The router does the work. Migrations run per-shard with dotnet ef database update --connection "$EACH" in CI — boring, scriptable, and the only sane way to keep shards in lockstep.

05The detail.

Virtual nodes save you from rebalance hell

The ring uses VirtualNodes = 200 per physical shard. Without virtual nodes, adding shard #5 moves a single contiguous arc — wildly unbalanced. With 200 vnodes, the new shard claims 200 small slices spread around the ring, each pulling a fraction of one existing shard's range. The variance drops from "one shard takes 40% of new load" to under 5%.

DbContextOptions are cached per shard, not per request

The ConcurrentDictionary in the factory caches a single options bag per shard ID. EF Core reuses it across requests, the underlying ADO.NET connection pool handles concurrency. Never new up DbContextOptionsBuilder per request — it bypasses the EF model cache and you'll pay model-build cost (50–200 ms) on every call.

Cross-shard queries are a smell

If a feature requires fan-out to every shard, that feature belongs in a read replica, an OLAP store, or an Elasticsearch index. The whole point of sharding is that each query touches one shard. Anything else is OLAP traffic wearing OLTP clothes.

Migrations need to be per-shard, not application-startup

The instinct to call db.Database.Migrate() in Program.cs dies the moment you have ten shards. Run migrations out-of-band, ideally as a deploy step that iterates shards in parallel with bounded concurrency. Track per-shard migration history so a failure halfway through doesn't leave you in a mixed state.

A second snippet — the migration runner you'd actually ship. Hosted service, parallel up to 4, per-shard retry, structured logging, fails the deploy on any single-shard failure:

C#   ShardMigrationRunner.cs 
using Microsoft.EntityFrameworkCore;
using Microsoft.Extensions.Hosting;
using Microsoft.Extensions.Logging;

namespace Academy.Sharding;

public sealed class ShardMigrationRunner : IHostedService
{
    private readonly IShardRouter _router;
    private readonly TenantDbContextFactory _factory;
    private readonly ILogger<ShardMigrationRunner> _log;
    private readonly IHostApplicationLifetime _lifetime;

    public ShardMigrationRunner(
        IShardRouter router,
        TenantDbContextFactory factory,
        ILogger<ShardMigrationRunner> log,
        IHostApplicationLifetime lifetime)
    {
        _router = router;
        _factory = factory;
        _log = log;
        _lifetime = lifetime;
    }

    public async Task StartAsync(CancellationToken ct)
    {
        using var sem = new SemaphoreSlim(4);
        var failed = new List<string>();

        var tasks = _router.All.Select(async shard =>
        {
            await sem.WaitAsync(ct);
            try
            {
                using var db = _factory.For(shard.Id); // any tenant key on this shard works
                _log.LogInformation("Migrating shard {Shard}", shard.Id);
                await db.Database.MigrateAsync(ct);
                _log.LogInformation("Shard {Shard} done", shard.Id);
            }
            catch (Exception ex)
            {
                _log.LogError(ex, "Shard {Shard} migration failed", shard.Id);
                lock (failed) failed.Add(shard.Id);
            }
            finally { sem.Release(); }
        });

        await Task.WhenAll(tasks);

        if (failed.Count > 0)
        {
            _log.LogCritical("Migration failed on {Count} shards: {List}",
                failed.Count, string.Join(",", failed));
            _lifetime.StopApplication(); // fail fast — orchestrator will not flip the rollout
        }
    }

    public Task StopAsync(CancellationToken ct) => Task.CompletedTask;
}

07Decision guide.

Under 100M rows, 5k TPS

One Postgres. Maybe read replicas. Stop reading articles about sharding and ship features.

B2B SaaS, hundreds of tenants

Directory shard by tenant. Citus on Postgres or one DB per tenant in an Azure SQL pool. Move noisy neighbours individually.

Multi-region writes, low ops headcount

Cosmos DB or Spanner. You're buying out of running the cluster. Budget for the RU bill — it's the real number.

Massive MySQL · YouTube-like scale

Vitess. Nothing else has the online-resharding maturity. You will pay for an SRE team.

Greenfield .NET, 30k+ TPS expected

Citus on Postgres + Marten if you need event sourcing. Shard key on the natural tenant column. Pre-allocate 1,024 logical shards.

Already in trouble, must reshard

Dual-write, backfill, verify, cut over. Two months minimum. Build the verifier on day one — it's the only thing that catches the bug that costs you the migration.

"The cheapest shard is the one you didn't add. The second cheapest is the one you pre-allocated three years ago and forgot about."
Three things people get wrong

One — sharding by something that isn't your access pattern. If 90% of queries are by user_id and you shard by created_at, every query is scatter-gather. Pick the shard key that matches the hot read path.

Two — believing the database will resharding itself. Cosmos DB and Citus are good. They are not "fire-and-forget for the next decade." Pre-allocate logical shards and watch hot-partition metrics like a hawk.

Three — running migrations from app startup. One shard times out, the whole rollout halts, you have a mixed-version production cluster. Out-of-band, parallel, idempotent. Always.
