"Drivers near me" at 1M RPS.

Every rider's phone polls "show me nearby drivers" every 4 seconds. With millions of riders, that's 1M+ spatial queries per second against millions of moving driver positions. Uber's solution was H3 — a hex-grid spatial index they built and open-sourced.

The constraint

Drivers move every second; positions go stale fast. Riders need updated results in milliseconds.

The move

H3 grid: world divided into hexagonal cells at multiple resolutions. Each driver's location is the cell ID at the chosen resolution.

Why hex, not square

Hexagons have only one distance metric (neighbour = same distance in all directions). Squares have two (orthogonal vs diagonal). Makes "find within radius" cleaner.

01The query at scale.

"Drivers within 2 km of (37.77, -122.4)" — easy in SQL: WHERE earth_distance(point) < 2000. Easy at 1k QPS; impossible at 1M QPS with 7M moving drivers. Drivers update positions every ~4 seconds; rider polls every 4 seconds; the database can't keep up with both the writes and the queries.

The architectural moves: (1) keep driver positions in memory, not SQL. (2) Use a spatial index that's cheap to maintain and cheap to query. (3) Shard by cell ID, not by driver ID.

"Geospatial at scale is a different category from CRUD. Different tools, different defaults."

02H3.

The world divided into hexagonal cells. 15 resolutions: resolution 0 is ~4.25M km² per hex (122 hexes covering the planet); resolution 15 is ~1 m². Every (lat, lon) maps to one hex ID per resolution, deterministically and cheaply (microseconds).

Index by cell

Each driver's location is their hex ID at resolution ~8 (~1 km² per cell). Look up "drivers in this hex" = O(1) hash lookup. Add the 6 neighbouring hexes for an ~3 km radius search. The query becomes 7 fast hash lookups.

Update is cheap

Driver moves. New position → new hex ID. Remove from old hex's set; add to new hex's set. Two map operations. Done. Compare to SQL: an UPDATE on an indexed table with B-tree maintenance.

Shard by hex prefix

The "geo service" is sharded by hex-ID prefix (effectively, by geographic region). San Francisco's drivers live on the SF shard; New York's on the NY shard. A rider's "nearby" query touches one shard. No global lookups.

03Hot-cell smoothing.

Times Square at 6pm: too many drivers in too few cells. The hot cell's read-write load swamps its shard. Sub-divide at higher resolution: when a cell exceeds a threshold, switch to resolution N+1 for that area; the load distributes across more cells. Adaptive grid resolution.

05The lessons.

Don't put hot geospatial in SQL

Postgres + PostGIS scales further than people think, but 1M QPS isn't its job. In-memory cell-indexed structures are 100× faster per query.

Hex grids beat squares for radius queries

One distance metric, uniform neighbour count, cleaner "ring N" semantics. H3 (Uber, open-source) and S2 (Google, open-source) are the standard libraries.

Shard by geography, not by entity

Drivers in SF live on the SF shard. Cross-region queries are rare. The shard key is the spatial index's prefix.

Adaptive resolution for hot zones

One hex grid resolution doesn't fit all density. Times Square needs finer cells than rural Wyoming. Switch resolutions per-region by load.

"The right data structure makes 1M QPS routine. The wrong one makes 1k QPS a Sev-1."
