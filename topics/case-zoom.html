10 million to 300 million in three months.

March 2020. Zoom's daily participant count went from ~10M to ~300M in 90 days. The infrastructure didn't melt. The story is half engineering investment they'd been making and half the SFU architecture that scales linearly with capacity.

SFU vs MCU

SFU (Selective Forwarding Unit) just relays streams between participants. MCU (Multi-point Control Unit) mixes streams server-side. Zoom uses SFU — clients do their own composition.

Why it scales

Server-side cost per participant is just forwarding video — not transcoding. A single SFU node handles hundreds of meetings.

01The 300M problem.

March 2020. Schools and offices closed; everyone went home; Zoom was the default. Daily participants: 10M → 200M in two months, 300M by April. Video calls are expensive: each participant uploads their video and downloads everyone else's. The server cost per participant is significant; the network bill scales linearly.

Most other video conferencing products got slow, dropped calls, or rate-limited. Zoom mostly didn't. The investment the team had been making for years — multi-DC fabric, the SFU architecture — paid off in the spike.

"You can't auto-scale your way out of 300 million users. You have to have already built the architecture."

02SFU — the architectural unlock.

Two competing architectures for multi-party video: MCU (server mixes all streams into one composite, sends one output per participant — server-CPU-heavy, low client bandwidth) and SFU (server forwards each stream to each other participant — server-CPU-light, higher client bandwidth).

Zoom uses SFU. The server's job is bandwidth, not CPU. Each SFU node handles hundreds of participants because forwarding is cheap. The client does the rendering — composing N streams into the grid view, encoding the local camera, decoding incoming streams.

Why SFU scales

For a meeting of N participants with simulcast: each participant uploads 1 stream; the SFU forwards (N-1) copies. Total server traffic: O(N²). For a 1:1 call, 2 streams. For a 100-person call, ~10,000 streams. Mitigation: video layers (different qualities); a participant only receives the layer they need (e.g., low-quality for the 30 people not currently speaking).

03Adaptive video.

Each participant uploads at multiple qualities (simulcast: e.g., 180p, 360p, 720p). The SFU forwards the quality each recipient can use based on their downlink. Recipient on a phone on 4G: 180p. Recipient on Wi-Fi: 720p. Speaker view gets the high-quality stream of the active speaker; gallery view gets low-quality streams of everyone.

Bandwidth adapts in real time. The recipient's client signals available bandwidth; the SFU adjusts which layer it forwards. No server-side transcoding (which would be expensive); the encoder did the work upfront.

05The lessons.

SFU is the modern multi-party video default

Pre-Zoom, MCU was common. SFU scales better and gives clients composition freedom. Almost every modern video conferencing product is SFU-based: Google Meet, Microsoft Teams, Discord, Twitch's video.

Simulcast is non-optional at scale

Encoding multiple qualities at the source means the server doesn't transcode. The cost is on the sender (modern devices handle it); the savings are on the server farm.

Architectural investment compounds during spikes

The Zoom team had been building this for 8 years before the pandemic. The 20× spike didn't break them because they'd already designed for headroom. You can't make this kind of investment under pressure — it's the work you do in the quiet years.

Multi-DC routing for global meetings

An attendee in Sydney joining a meeting hosted by an attendee in NY shouldn't traverse the Pacific. Zoom routes to the SFU nearest the participant, with cross-DC fabric for streams that need to cross.

"Capacity is a build decision, not a deploy decision."
