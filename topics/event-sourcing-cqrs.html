Store the decisions. Derive the rest.

A row in a database is a summary. The events that produced it are gone. Event sourcing keeps the events as the source of truth and rebuilds the row on demand. Pair it with CQRS — separate write and read models — and you get an audit log, time travel, and projection-as-a-feature, in exchange for one of the steeper learning curves in the field.

The problem in one line

"Why is this customer's balance $43.21?" — a CRUD database can show you the row, but not the sequence of facts that produced it. That sequence is the business.

The thesis

Append-only event log is the write model. Projections build whatever read models the queries need. Rebuild the read side without touching the write side. The events are the contract.

01What CRUD throws away.

A CRUD database stores the result of decisions. A customer's Balance = 43.21 column tells you the current state. It doesn't tell you that the balance was 50.00, then a charge for 10.00 arrived, then a refund of 3.21 was applied, then a fee of 0.00 was logged and reverted because it was a bug. The facts are the events; the row is the summary. Every UPDATE destroys information the business may later need.

The traditional workaround is the audit table — a parallel structure that records every change. Audit tables are unloved, frequently divergent from the truth, and rebuilt every two years when somebody discovers nobody trusts them anymore. Event sourcing inverts the trust: the audit log is the database, and the "current state" is the materialised view. The trick that took fifteen years to land in mainstream stacks is that this isn't more storage — disks are cheap — it's a different relationship to your data.

"The state is a function of the events. Lose the events, lose the truth."

The decision to event-source is binary at the aggregate level. You event-source orders; you don't event-source the search index. The aggregates whose history is the business — accounts, orders, policies, prescriptions, claims — earn the cost. The aggregates that are derived — product catalogs, configuration, recommendations — don't. If you can't articulate why your bounded context needs the history, don't event-source it.

02Events, streams, projections.

Three primitives, in order of architectural weight: events (immutable facts), streams (events about one aggregate, in order), projections (anything derived).

Fig. 13The same event stream produces every read shape the business needs. New shape? New projection. The write model never changes.

CQRS is the natural pair

Command Query Responsibility Segregation says the model for writing and the model for reading should be different. Event sourcing forces it: the write model is "append an event to a stream"; the read model is "query a projection". The schemas don't match. They shouldn't. The orders write side has one shape; the customer-facing order list view has a different shape; the back-office reconciliation report has a third shape; the search index has a fourth. Trying to serve all four from one normalised SQL schema is what kills relational designs at scale.

Projections are not transactional with writes

An event lands in the stream. The Order Summary projection is updated 50 milliseconds later. The read side is eventually consistent. A user who just placed an order may not see it in their list immediately. The workaround for that specific UX is to return the projected entity from the command handler — read your own writes by re-projecting in-process — or to read from the stream directly for the post-command refresh. Beyond that, the business has to accept the lag. The lag is the price; not all systems can pay it.

03Snapshots and versioning.

Two operational realities that the textbook skips: rebuilding the aggregate from 10,000 events is slow, and your events evolve over the codebase's lifetime. Both have solved patterns; both have to be built in from day one.

Snapshots: cache the state, not the truth

To rebuild an aggregate, you replay all its events. For long-lived aggregates — a customer with five years of activity — that's thousands of events on every command. The fix is a snapshot: every N events (typically 100), serialise the aggregate's state and store it next to the stream. Rebuild becomes "load the latest snapshot, then replay only events newer than the snapshot." Snapshots are an optimisation, not a source of truth. If a snapshot is corrupt or stale, the events still rebuild correctly; you just rebuild slowly until the next snapshot.

Marten handles snapshots transparently. EventStoreDB does too. Roll-your-own event stores need to remember that snapshots must invalidate cleanly on aggregate-schema changes — otherwise you replay against an old shape and the deserialiser explodes at 3am.

Event versioning is your most permanent decision

An event was written in 2022 with a currency: string field. In 2024, you split it into currencyCode and currencyExponent. The 2022 events are still in the stream — they're immutable facts. Three patterns to deal with this, in order of pain:

Upcasters: when an old event is loaded, transform it to the new shape on the way in. Code stays clean; deserialisation gets a hook. Marten's Schema.For<TEvent>().Upcaster(...) handles this. Versioned event types: OrderPlacedV1 and OrderPlacedV2 co-exist as distinct types; the projection handles both. Verbose but unambiguous. Migration rewrite: replay the stream, write a new stream with corrected events, swap. Use only for breaking changes you cannot upcast — and accept that the new stream is the new truth.

Idempotent projections

Projections will be re-run. A bug is found, the projection is reset, the stream is replayed. The projection must produce the same read model from the same events every time. No external side-effects in a projection — no emails, no Slack alerts, no third-party API calls. Those are handled by separate event handlers that track their own "have I processed this event id" idempotency keys. The projection's job is to write to a SQL table or a search index, with idempotent UPSERTs. That's the contract.

04In Marten.

Marten is the most pragmatic .NET event store: it's a Postgres extension. The same database that holds your projection tables holds your event log, transactionally. No second cluster to operate. The aggregate is a class that knows how to fold events into state; the projection is a class that mirrors that fold into a read table.

C#   OrderAggregate.cs 
// dotnet add package Marten
using Marten.Events.Aggregation;

namespace Academy.Orders;

// Events — append-only facts. Immutable records.
public sealed record OrderPlaced(Guid OrderId, Guid CustomerId, decimal Amount);
public sealed record PaymentAuthorised(Guid OrderId, Guid PaymentId);
public sealed record StockReserved(Guid OrderId, Guid ReservationId);
public sealed record ShipmentBooked(Guid OrderId, string Carrier, string Tracking);
public sealed record OrderCompleted(Guid OrderId);
public sealed record OrderCancelled(Guid OrderId, string Reason);

// Aggregate state — folded from events. Pure functions, no side effects.
public sealed class Order
{
    public Guid    Id          { get; set; }
    public Guid    CustomerId  { get; set; }
    public decimal Amount      { get; set; }
    public string  Status      { get; set; } = "Pending";
    public Guid?   PaymentId   { get; set; }
    public Guid?   ReservationId { get; set; }
    public string? Tracking    { get; set; }

    public void Apply(OrderPlaced e)
        { Id = e.OrderId; CustomerId = e.CustomerId; Amount = e.Amount; }

    public void Apply(PaymentAuthorised e) { PaymentId = e.PaymentId; Status = "Paid"; }
    public void Apply(StockReserved e)     { ReservationId = e.ReservationId; }
    public void Apply(ShipmentBooked e)    { Tracking = e.Tracking; Status = "Shipped"; }
    public void Apply(OrderCompleted e)    { Status = "Completed"; }
    public void Apply(OrderCancelled e)    { Status = "Cancelled"; }
}

// Read model — built by a projection from the same events.
public sealed class OrderSummary
{
    public Guid Id { get; set; }
    public string Status { get; set; } = "";
    public decimal Total  { get; set; }
    public string? Tracking { get; set; }
    public DateTimeOffset LastUpdated { get; set; }
}

public sealed class OrderSummaryProjection : SingleStreamProjection<OrderSummary>
{
    public OrderSummary Create(OrderPlaced e) => new()
        { Id = e.OrderId, Total = e.Amount, Status = "Pending", LastUpdated = DateTimeOffset.UtcNow };

    public void Apply(PaymentAuthorised _, OrderSummary s) { s.Status = "Paid"; s.LastUpdated = DateTimeOffset.UtcNow; }
    public void Apply(ShipmentBooked e,   OrderSummary s) { s.Status = "Shipped"; s.Tracking = e.Tracking; }
    public void Apply(OrderCompleted _,   OrderSummary s) { s.Status = "Completed"; }
    public void Apply(OrderCancelled _,   OrderSummary s) { s.Status = "Cancelled"; }
}

The command handler appends events; Marten persists them and runs the projection inline (or async, configurable per projection). The append is transactional with the projection update — same Postgres database, same SQL transaction.

C#   Program.cs 
var builder = WebApplication.CreateBuilder(args);

builder.Services.AddMarten(opts =>
{
    opts.Connection(builder.Configuration.GetConnectionString("Postgres")!);
    opts.Events.StreamIdentity = StreamIdentity.AsGuid;

    opts.Projections.Add<OrderSummaryProjection>(ProjectionLifecycle.Inline);

    // Snapshot every 100 events; rebuild safely from the stream.
    opts.Events.AddEventType(typeof(OrderPlaced));
    opts.Schema.For<Order>().Identity(o => o.Id);
}).UseLightweightSessions();

var app = builder.Build();

app.MapPost("/orders", async (PlaceOrderDto req, IDocumentSession s, CancellationToken ct) =>
{
    var id = Guid.NewGuid();
    var placed = new OrderPlaced(id, req.CustomerId, req.Amount);
    s.Events.StartStream<Order>(id, placed);
    await s.SaveChangesAsync(ct);
    return Results.Created($"/orders/{id}", new { id });
});

app.MapPost("/orders/{id:guid}/pay", async (
    Guid id, PayDto req, IDocumentSession s, CancellationToken ct) =>
{
    // Optimistic concurrency: replay current state, validate, append.
    var order = await s.Events.AggregateStreamAsync<Order>(id, token: ct);
    if (order is null)            return Results.NotFound();
    if (order.Status != "Pending") return Results.Conflict();

    s.Events.Append(id, new PaymentAuthorised(id, req.PaymentId));
    await s.SaveChangesAsync(ct);
    return Results.NoContent();
});

app.MapGet("/orders/{id:guid}", async (Guid id, IQuerySession s) =>
    (await s.LoadAsync<OrderSummary>(id)) is { } o ? Results.Ok(o) : Results.NotFound());

app.Run();

05The detail.

Events are past-tense facts

Name events in the past tense: OrderPlaced, PaymentAuthorised, never PlaceOrder or AuthorisePayment (those are commands). The distinction is load-bearing: a command can be rejected, an event cannot. Once written, the event is the fact. The verb-tense rule is the first place naming drift shows up.

Inline vs async projections

Marten lets you mark a projection Inline (updated in the same transaction as the event append) or Async (updated by a background daemon). Inline gives you read-your-writes consistency at the cost of write throughput. Async gives you scale at the cost of staleness. Inline for the projection that powers the same user's next screen, async for everything else — search indexes, reporting tables, audit trails.

Optimistic concurrency on the stream

Two commands arrive for the same order at the same time. Both load state, both validate, both try to append. Without concurrency control, both succeed and the second one's "validation against current state" was actually against stale state. Marten's AppendExpectedVersion handles it: the append fails if the stream has moved since you read it, and you re-execute the command against fresh state.

Don't event-source everything

The customer's order history? Event-source. The list of countries? Don't. The recommendation cache? Don't. Event sourcing earns its keep where the history is the business. A reference data table that changes once a quarter is just a table. Mixing event-sourced aggregates with plain document storage in the same Marten database is normal and recommended.

Replay is your debugging tool

A bug in the projection? Reset the read model, replay the stream, you get the corrected state. A new requirement needs a new view? Write a new projection, run it from event 0, you get the new view from the dawn of time. This is the operational superpower of event sourcing, and the reason teams who get past the learning curve never want to go back.

"A CRUD migration is a one-way trip. An event-sourced migration is a function you rerun until it's right."

07Decision guide.

Domain where history IS the business

Finance, healthcare, supply chain, legal. Event-source the aggregates. The audit log isn't a feature — it's the database.

.NET on Postgres, <10k events/sec

Marten. One database, transactional projections, snapshot support. The lowest-friction onramp in the ecosystem.

>10k events/sec or polyglot

EventStoreDB. Purpose-built, language-neutral, built-in subscriptions and projections. The operational cost is the price of the throughput.

Read-heavy domain with many shapes

Pair ES with CQRS aggressively. Build a projection per query shape. Don't try to make one normalised SQL schema serve every view.

CRUD domain with audit requirement

Don't event-source. Use change data capture (Chapter 17) or temporal tables. ES is overkill for "we need to know who changed what."

Greenfield app, undecided

Start with a modular monolith (Chapter 12) and CRUD. Add ES only to the aggregates where history is asked of you by name. Mixed models are normal.

Three things people get wrong

One — event-sourcing the whole system. Treating event sourcing as an architecture, not a tactic, leaks the learning curve into every team. Reserve it for the aggregates whose history is asked of you by the business.

Two — using events as messages. An event is a fact about one aggregate. A cross-aggregate message is a different artefact and belongs on a broker, not in the event store. Conflating the two is the most common shape-bug in early ES designs.

Three — never planning for event versioning. The first event-schema change comes within six months. Without an upcaster strategy from day one, you'll be writing migration scripts that "rewrite the past" — and getting the audit trail wrong.
