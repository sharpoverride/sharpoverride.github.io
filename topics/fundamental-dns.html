The world's largest cache hierarchy.

DNS is a global hierarchy of caches in front of a tiny authoritative truth. A lookup almost never reaches the authoritative servers; it terminates at one of millions of recursive caches scattered across the internet. That's why it's fast — and that's why TTLs are the most important number nobody on your team knows.

The three actors

Authoritative: owns the records. Recursive resolver: queries on your behalf, caches aggressively. Stub resolver: the one in your OS, asks the recursive.

The trick

Almost every lookup hits a cache somewhere on the path. The authoritative servers are visited rarely. DNS is fast not because it's smart but because it's lazy.

01The naming problem.

Computers connect by IP address. Humans connect by names. The mapping has to be global, fast, and let a domain owner change their IP without re-issuing business cards to the world. DNS is that mapping: a hierarchical, cache-friendly, eventually-consistent key-value store at planetary scale.

The clever shape is the hierarchy. One root, ~1500 TLDs, 350M+ second-level domains. Each level only knows where to find the next. The root knows about the TLDs (.com, .org); .com's servers know about example.com; example.com's servers know about www.example.com. No single authority knows everything; every authority knows enough to delegate further.

"DNS works because nobody owns it. Or rather: many somebodies each own a tiny piece of it."

02The hierarchy.

Fig. 25One uncached lookup is 4 round-trips. Every layer caches. Most lookups in production traverse one — the stub-to-recursive — and the recursive answers from cache.

Record types you'll meet

A / AAAA: hostname → IPv4 / IPv6 address. The most common record. CNAME: an alias to another hostname; resolves to the target's A. MX: mail server for the domain. TXT: arbitrary text, used for SPF, DKIM, domain-verification challenges. NS: which authoritative servers serve this domain. SRV: service discovery for non-HTTP protocols. You'll spend 90% of your time with A and CNAME.

03TTLs and propagation.

Every record has a TTL — how long downstream caches may hold it before refreshing. A 300-second TTL means a recursive resolver will serve the cached answer for 5 minutes, then re-fetch. Pick the TTL based on how often you change the record: low for things that fail over (60s for production load-balancer IPs), high for things that never change (24h for vanity domain CNAMEs).

"DNS propagation" is the TTL of your old record

When you change an A record, the change is instant at the authoritative servers. But every recursive resolver in the world has the old answer cached for as long as the old TTL says. If the old TTL was 24 hours, you wait 24 hours for the world to converge on the new record. The standard trick before a known cutover: lower the TTL to 60 seconds 24 hours in advance. The cache fills with short-TTL versions; the cutover then propagates in a minute.

Negative caching

If a name doesn't exist, the resolver caches the "doesn't exist" answer too — typically for 5 minutes by default, controlled by the SOA record's negative TTL. This is why a typo'd domain returning NXDOMAIN keeps returning NXDOMAIN for a few minutes even after you fix the typo: the resolver remembers your error.

Health-aware DNS

Managed DNS providers (Route 53, Azure DNS, Cloudflare, NS1) tie records to health checks. If the IP behind your A record fails its health probe, the provider returns a different IP. This is the most common cross-region failover mechanism — works for any TCP-based service, not just HTTP. Caveat: the failover speed is bounded by the TTL.

04In .NET / production.

The most important .NET DNS configuration is one line: PooledConnectionLifetime on HttpClient's handler. Without it, the HttpClient never re-resolves DNS — see Service Discovery.

C#   HttpClientFactorySetup.cs 
builder.Services.AddHttpClient<PaymentsClient>(c =>
{
    c.BaseAddress = new Uri("https://payments.internal");
})
.ConfigurePrimaryHttpMessageHandler(() => new SocketsHttpHandler
{
    // Most important DNS config in .NET. Force re-resolve every 2 min.
    PooledConnectionLifetime    = TimeSpan.FromMinutes(2),
    PooledConnectionIdleTimeout = TimeSpan.FromMinutes(5),
});

DNS for K8s and ECS

Inside a Kubernetes cluster, CoreDNS resolves service.namespace.svc.cluster.local. The TTL defaults to 30 seconds. Inside ECS, AWS Cloud Map (or Route 53) does the same. Cluster DNS is real DNS; the same caching, TTL, and propagation rules apply — see Service Discovery.

05The detail.

Lower TTL before a cutover

24 hours before flipping an A record, drop the TTL to 60 seconds. Caches age out. Day-of, you can switch in a minute. Restore the TTL to its normal value after.

CNAME at the apex is forbidden

RFC says example.com can't be a CNAME (the apex must be SOA + NS). Solutions: ALIAS records (Cloudflare, Route 53) that the DNS provider resolves and returns as A records; or use www.example.com as the canonical and redirect from apex.

Use 1.1.1.1 or 8.8.8.8 as your debug resolver

When debugging DNS issues, query a known-good public resolver (dig @1.1.1.1 example.com) to bypass your local resolver. If the answer is correct there but wrong at your stub, the bug is in your network's resolver chain.

DNSSEC: real, niche, often broken

DNSSEC signs records cryptographically. It prevents poisoning attacks. It's also infamous for breaking domains when keys rotate incorrectly — high-profile outages have hit organisations that enabled it. Use it for compliance-sensitive domains; for general use, the additional security is small and the operational risk is real.

DoH / DoT encrypts the lookup

DNS-over-HTTPS and DNS-over-TLS encrypt the resolver-to-recursive traffic. Modern browsers default to DoH (Cloudflare or NextDNS or whatever the OS prefers). Your corporate network monitoring won't see DNS lookups by default; if you rely on them for security, the policy needs to push enterprise-DNS configuration to the endpoints.

"DNS is the protocol everyone thinks is simple until they ship a TTL of 86400 the day before a launch."

06Decision guide.

Hosting a domain

Cloudflare DNS or Route 53. Both are fast, well-engineered, well-priced. Pick the one matching your cloud unless Cloudflare's free tier wins.

Health-based failover

Route 53 health checks or Cloudflare load balancer. TTL 30–60s. Failover happens in ~1 minute after detection.

Multi-region geographic routing

Route 53 latency policies, Azure Traffic Manager performance routing, or Cloudflare's geo-routing. Routes user to nearest healthy region.

Inside Kubernetes

CoreDNS handles it. Set the default 30s TTL; HttpClient.PooledConnectionLifetime in .NET clients is the matching configuration.

Big migration coming

Lower the TTL on the migrated records to 60s, 24h before the cutover. Restore after. Don't try to cut over with the production TTL still set.

You think DNS is causing slowness

Probably right. Check HttpClient connection lifetime, DNS resolver health, and stub-resolver TTL. The fix is usually one config line.
