Fundamental deep dive Difficulty ★★★★★ Read ~13 min Stack Matching engine · disruptor · LMAX · FIX

Microseconds cost money.

A stock exchange is the canonical low-latency system: process millions of orders per second, match them deterministically, broadcast results to thousands of market participants — all in single-digit microseconds. The LMAX Disruptor paper is the most-cited piece of architectural folklore in this space.

The constraints

Deterministic matching. Sub-microsecond latency. Millions of orders/sec. Audit log of every event. Fair ordering (don't favour high-frequency traders).

The architectural moves

Single-threaded matching engine. Lock-free queues (Disruptor). Pre-allocated everything. Kernel bypass for network I/O. Co-location at the exchange.

01Why micros matter.

A high-frequency trading firm's profit depends on hitting the market before its competitors. If you're 100 μs slower, you eat their dust on price-improving orders. The exchange itself is the arena; participants pay for co-location (their server racks next to the matching engine) and direct fibre links. The exchange's job is fairness: each order's time-of-arrival is the only thing that should affect outcome.

The systems-design question: build a matcher that's deterministic, fast, fair, and audit-grade durable. The architectural answer turns out to be unusually old-school — single-threaded, lock-free, RAM-resident.

"In a stock exchange, sequential is the new parallel."

02The matching engine.

Order book

Per-symbol: two priority queues. Bids (descending price). Asks (ascending price). An incoming order is matched against the opposite side while prices cross; the unmatched remainder sits on the book at its price/time.

Price-time priority

Best price first; for orders at the same price, earliest first. Time ordering is what makes the exchange fair. If two HFT firms send orders 1 μs apart, the earlier one fills first.

Single-threaded by design

Multi-threading introduces non-determinism. Two threads matching in parallel can produce different orderings depending on scheduling. The matching engine is single-threaded — deterministic, replay-able, simpler. Throughput is high because per-order processing is microseconds; sequential throughput is millions of orders per second.

03LMAX Disruptor.

LMAX was a UK exchange whose 2010 paper named the pattern. The Disruptor is a lock-free ring buffer: producers and consumers coordinate via cache-line-aware sequence counters. No locks, no garbage collection (pre-allocated objects), CPU-cache-friendly.

The pipeline

StageWhat it does
ReceiverNetwork → deserialise → enqueue
JournallerWrite to durable log (for replay / audit)
ReplicatorSend to standby replica
UnmarshallerDecode message
Business logicMatch against order book
MarshallerEncode response
PublisherSend to subscribers

Each stage is single-threaded; they communicate through ring buffers. The whole pipeline runs entirely in CPU cache once warmed. Per-order latency: ~1 μs through the entire chain.

Determinism enables replay

The journaller writes every input event. If the matching engine crashes, replay the events from the log; you get exactly the same order book state. No "fuzz" because there's no concurrency. The audit log is the database.

04The network path.

Kernel bypass

Normal socket I/O goes through the kernel: syscalls, TCP stack, copies. Each layer adds microseconds. Stock exchanges use kernel bypass (Solarflare OpenOnload, DPDK, RDMA) where the NIC writes packets directly into userspace memory. Latency: ~5 μs instead of ~50 μs.

FIX protocol

Financial Information eXchange. The standard message format for orders. ASCII-based originally; modern FIX uses FAST or SBE (Simple Binary Encoding) for performance. Encode in microseconds; transmit in microseconds; decode in microseconds.

Multicast for market data

The exchange publishes order-book updates to thousands of subscribers. Unicast doesn't scale (N copies of every update). UDP multicast: send once, the network duplicates. Plus a gap-fill TCP channel for clients who miss a packet.

05Decision guide.

Building anything HFT-shaped

Disruptor pattern. Single-threaded matching. Pre-allocated objects. Kernel bypass. Co-locate physically.

Crypto exchange (lower latency tolerance)

You can get away with multi-threaded if you serialise carefully. Most crypto exchanges are in millisecond-territory, not microsecond.

Order book in your app (matching, not exchange)

Same data structure (two priority queues). Concurrency is fine; you don't have HFT fairness requirements.

Replication / disaster recovery

Replay the event log on the standby. Standby is "warm" — it has applied every event but doesn't matchings. On failover, it picks up where the primary left off.

Multicast on commodity cloud

Doesn't work — major clouds don't support multicast natively. Either run on bare-metal or use overlay multicast (XDP, custom UDP fanout).

Low-latency without being an exchange

Ad bidding, real-time analytics, gaming servers. The Disruptor pattern transfers. The kernel-bypass story doesn't unless you really need it.

"The architecture is unusual not because finance is hard, but because microseconds are real money."
