Do you actually need multi-region?

The architecture review wants two regions. The customer wants 50ms latency. The CFO wants the bill to stay one digit lower. The honest question is which of these constraints is binding — and the answer is rarely all three at once.

The problem in one line

Light travels 200ms round-trip from Sydney to London. You cannot solve geographic latency with more replicas. You solve it by putting the data closer.

The thesis

Active/passive is the right default. Active/active is correct only when single-region latency is unacceptable AND your data tolerates conflict resolution. Spanner-class strong-consistency-with-distance is the most expensive correct answer.

01Why multi-region is the wrong default.

Two reasons people add a second region: disaster recovery ("what if the primary region goes down") and latency ("we have customers in Tokyo"). They are different problems. The first is solved by a passive replica you fail over to. The second is solved by serving reads — and possibly writes — from a region close to the user. Conflating them produces architectures that are expensive for both.

The honest baseline: a single Azure or AWS region has >99.99% uptime by SLA. Most outages people remember as "the cloud went down" were single-zone failures in a single region, mitigated by multi-AZ rather than multi-region. Going multi-region adds a 2–3× cost for the second region plus the operational complexity of cross-region replication, conflict handling, and failover testing. Do it because you measured a real problem, not because the architecture review demanded it.

"You don't earn multi-region. You inherit the bill for it the next quarter, every quarter, forever."

02Three shapes.

By increasing cost and complexity: active/passive, active/active by partition, active/active fully replicated.

Fig. 20Each topology trades a different pair of CAP letters. Partitioning sidesteps conflicts entirely — it's the most often correct, least often used pattern.

Active/passive: what most people actually need

One region serves all traffic. A second region runs continuously with replicated data, ready to take over. The replication is async, so the passive region lags by milliseconds to seconds. Failover is a deliberate operation — promote the passive, point DNS at it, accept a brief outage. RPO (data loss) is the replication lag at the moment of failure; RTO (time to recover) is your failover runbook's wall-clock. A well-rehearsed failover happens in 5–15 minutes; an unrehearsed one is a panic-call from your CTO.

Active/active by partition: the underrated answer

European customers' data lives in europe-west. American customers' data lives in us-east. Each region has the same code, the same schema, but disjoint datasets. There are no replication conflicts because there is no cross-region write contention — a row exists in exactly one region. This is the topology Salesforce, Shopify, and Stripe use for tenanted SaaS, and it neatly aligns with GDPR data-residency. The downside: a customer that moves countries requires a tenant migration; rare cross-tenant queries become harder.

Active/active fully replicated: when you really need it

Every region accepts writes for the same data. Cosmos DB multi-master, DynamoDB Global Tables, Aurora Global Database (read replicas only, but Aurora DSQL is changing that). The tech works; the application has to handle conflicts. This is the most operationally complex shape and is rarely what you actually need. The use case that justifies it: a globally consistent shopping cart, a global user profile, a global session — anything where the same logical record is written from multiple regions in a short window. Even there, "stickiness by hash of user-id" often gives you partition-style isolation with less pain.

03Conflict resolution.

Two regions accept a write to the same record in the same 100ms. Replication brings them together. The database has to pick. Four strategies, in order of correctness:

Last-write-wins (LWW)

Each write carries a timestamp; the one with the larger timestamp wins. Cosmos DB's default. Easy to implement, easy to get wrong. Clock skew between regions can be 10–50ms; a logically-later write can appear chronologically earlier and lose. LWW works fine for use cases where "the data is whatever the most recent thing the user did" is acceptable — user preferences, last-seen-at timestamps, theme settings. It is wrong for counters, balances, or anything that aggregates.

Custom resolver function

Cosmos and DynamoDB let you register a stored-procedure-like function that runs on conflict: "given the two conflicting versions, return the merged result." For a counter, sum the deltas. For a cart, take the union. For an inventory level, take the minimum. This is where most real conflicts get handled. The function is application-specific; the database can't infer the merge for you.

CRDTs (Conflict-free Replicated Data Types)

A data structure that is mathematically guaranteed to converge regardless of write order. Counters (G-Counter, PN-Counter), sets (G-Set, OR-Set), maps (Map-of-Maps). Azure Cosmos DB's "counter" type is a CRDT under the hood. If your data fits a CRDT, you've removed conflict from the architecture by construction. Riak and Redis Enterprise also expose CRDT types. Collaborative editing (Figma, Linear) is built on CRDTs end-to-end.

Strong consistency (Spanner, Cosmos Strong)

The database refuses to acknowledge a write until a quorum across regions has accepted it. Google Spanner uses GPS+atomic clocks ("TrueTime") to bound clock skew, then runs a Paxos quorum across regions. Writes pay the cross-region round-trip on every commit — typically 50–150ms. Strong consistency over distance is correctness at the cost of latency you cannot get back. Use it when "two simultaneous writes returning different values" is a regulatory or financial bug; avoid it for everything else.

04In .NET.

Cosmos DB is the most common multi-region database in the .NET ecosystem. The client routes reads and writes to the nearest region; the database handles replication. Front Door does the front-end traffic routing. The interesting code is the configuration — not the application code.

C#   CosmosMultiRegion.cs 
// dotnet add package Microsoft.Azure.Cosmos
using Microsoft.Azure.Cosmos;

namespace Academy.Geo;

public sealed class OrderRepository
{
    private readonly Container _orders;

    public OrderRepository(CosmosClient cosmos)
    { _orders = cosmos.GetDatabase("shop").GetContainer("orders"); }

    // Reads automatically go to the closest replica region (preferred read locations).
    public async Task<Order?> GetAsync(Guid id, string customerId, CancellationToken ct)
    {
        try
        {
            var resp = await _orders.ReadItemAsync<Order>(
                id: id.ToString(),
                partitionKey: new PartitionKey(customerId),
                cancellationToken: ct);
            return resp.Resource;
        }
        catch (CosmosException ex) when (ex.StatusCode == HttpStatusCode.NotFound)
        { return null; }
    }

    // Writes go to the nearest write-enabled region (multi-master) or the single hub region.
    public async Task UpsertAsync(Order order, CancellationToken ct)
    {
        await _orders.UpsertItemAsync(order,
            partitionKey: new PartitionKey(order.CustomerId),
            cancellationToken: ct);
    }
}

// Configure the CosmosClient at startup with regional preferences and a conflict resolver.
public static class CosmosRegistration
{
    public static IServiceCollection AddMultiRegionCosmos(
        this IServiceCollection services, IConfiguration cfg)
    {
        var options = new CosmosClientOptions
        {
            ConnectionMode = ConnectionMode.Direct,
            ApplicationRegion = cfg["Azure:Region"],     // what region this pod is in
            ApplicationPreferredRegions = cfg
                .GetSection("Cosmos:PreferredRegions").Get<List<string>>(),
            ConsistencyLevel = ConsistencyLevel.Session,   // read your writes per session
            MaxRetryAttemptsOnRateLimitedRequests = 9,
            MaxRetryWaitTimeOnRateLimitedRequests    = TimeSpan.FromSeconds(30),
        };

        services.AddSingleton(_ => new CosmosClient(
            cfg.GetConnectionString("Cosmos")!, options));

        services.AddScoped<OrderRepository>();
        return services;
    }
}

Traffic routing: Front Door (or Traffic Manager) decides which region serves the incoming request. Front Door is L7 with global anycast and TLS termination at the edge; Traffic Manager is DNS-based and cheaper but coarser. For most apps, Front Door is the right choice — anycast latency wins, and the integrated WAF is included.

05The detail.

Test failover. Monthly. In production.

A failover plan you haven't executed is a failover plan you don't have. Schedule a real cutover quarterly. Chaos Monkey for regions — pick a region, fail it, see what happens. The runbook that hasn't been used is wrong.

Egress is the cost line nobody budgets

Inter-region data transfer on Azure is ~$0.02–0.04 per GB. A multi-region app pushing 10 TB/month of replication traffic is ~$300/mo in egress alone, separate from compute and storage. Plan for it; the bill shows up at month-end and surprises CFOs.

Session consistency is the right Cosmos default

ConsistencyLevel.Session guarantees a client reads its own writes and reads in monotonic order. It costs nothing extra. Strong is the wrong default for multi-region — every write pays cross-region latency. Eventual is the wrong default for app code — your user clicks "save", refreshes, doesn't see their change, files a bug.

Failover is more than DNS

Sticky session state, in-flight WebSockets (see Chapter 10), open SQL transactions, in-memory caches — none of these survive a region failover. The app has to be designed for the warm region to start cold and the cold region to take 30–60 seconds to be ready for traffic. Capacity in the passive region must be pre-warmed or you'll cold-start through the outage.

Partitioning by tenant is the cheap win

If your customers are tenanted (B2B SaaS), pin each tenant to a region. EU customers in West Europe, US customers in East US. Reads and writes both stay local. No conflicts. GDPR-clean. It's the topology Stripe, Shopify, Salesforce use and the one most B2C apps should consider before reaching for multi-master.

"Latency is a function of distance and the speed of light. Architecture cannot beat physics."

07Decision guide.

"We need DR" — single region today

Active/passive in a second region. Replica running, traffic not. Quarterly failover drill. The cheapest correct DR posture.

B2B SaaS with EU + US customers

Partition by tenant region. Pin each customer to their nearest region. No replication conflicts; GDPR happy.

B2C, global user base, sticky sessions OK

Hash the user-id to a home region; route their traffic and data there. Effectively partition-by-user.

Global shopping cart, true multi-writer

Cosmos multi-master or DynamoDB global tables with a custom conflict resolver. Be honest about the resolver complexity.

Cross-region ACID transactions

Spanner. Accept the latency. There is no second option that gives you the same semantics.

"The architecture review asked for multi-region"

Ask which constraint is binding: DR, latency, or compliance. Pick the topology that answers that constraint; don't conflate them.

Three things people get wrong

One — conflating DR with latency. Active/passive solves DR. Active/active solves latency. They are different costs, different complexities, different patterns. Pick the topology for the problem you actually have.

Two — assuming LWW is safe. LWW silently loses data on concurrent writes. Counters, balances, anything aggregated needs CRDT or a custom resolver — not LWW.

Three — never failing over. An untested failover is a fictional failover. The drill is the architecture; the diagram is the marketing. Run the drill.
